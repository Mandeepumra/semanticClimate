4.4.3.3
New information becomes available
1
The science on climate change, its impacts and the opportunities to mitigate is continuously being
2
updated. Even though decisions are no longer made “in a sea of uncertainty” (Lave 1991), we know
3
that new information will come over time, that may have significant bearing on the design and
4
objectives of policies to shift development pathways and accelerate mitigation. New information may
5
come from climate sciences (e.g., updated GWP values or available carbon budgets) (Quéré et al. 2018),
6
impact sciences (e.g., re-evaluation of climate impacts associated with given emission pathways) (Ricke
7
et al. 2018) or mitigation sciences (e.g., on availability of given technologies) (Lenzi et al. 2018;
8
Giannousakis et al. 2020).
9
At the same time, economic and social systems are characterised by high degree of inertia, via long-
10
lived capital stock or urban forms (Lecocq and Shalizi 2014), or more broadly mutually reinforcing
11
physical, economic, and social constraints (Seto et al. 2016) that may lead to carbon lock-ins (Erickson
12
et al. 2015). Risks associated with long-lasting fossil-fuel power plants have been the object of particular
13
attention. For example, Pfeiffer et al. (2018) estimate that even if the current pipeline of power plants
14
was cancelled, about 20% of the existing capacity might be stranded to remain compatible with 1.5°C
15
or 2°C pathways—implying that additional capital accumulation would lead to higher sunk costs
16
associated with stranded assets (Luderer et al. 2018b; Johnson et al. 2015; Ansar et al. 2013; Kriegler
17
et al. 2018).
18
In the presence of uncertainty and inertia (or irreversibilities), hedging strategies may be considered,
19
that include selection of risk-hedging strategies and processes to adjust decisions as new information
20
becomes available. The notion of hedging against risks is also prominent in the adaptation literature, as
21
exemplified by the terminology of “climate resilient development” (Fankhauser and McDermott 2016)
22
(WGII, Ch.18). There is also a growing literature on hedging strategies for individual actors (e.g., firms
23
or investors) in the face of the uncertainties associated with mitigation (e.g., policy uncertainty or the
24
associated carbon price uncertainty) (e.g., (Morris et al. 2018) or (Andersson et al. 2016)). On the other
25
hand, there is often limited discussion of uncertainty and of its implication for hedging strategies in the
26
accelerated mitigation pathway literature. Exceptions include (Capros et al. 2019), who elicit “no-
27
regret” and “disruptive” mitigation options for the EU through a detailed sensitivity analysis, and
28
(Watson et al. 2015) who discuss flexible strategies for the U.K. energy sector transition in the face of
29
multiple uncertainties.

4.4.3.4
Black swans (e.g., COVID-19 crisis)
31
As the current COVID-19 crisis demonstrates, events happen that can derail the best-laid plans.
32
Unexpected events beyond the range of human experience until then are called ‘black swans’, given the
33
expectation that all swans are white. The only point to note here is that such events may also provide
34
opportunities. In the COVID-19 case, for example, the experience of conducting many activities on-
35
line, which reduces emissions from transport, may leave an imprint on how some of these activities are
36
carried out in the post-COVID-19 world. Similarly, reduced air pollution seen during the pandemics
37
may increase support for mitigation and strengthen the case for climate action. However, the emissions
38
implications of recovery packages depend on choosing policies that support climate action while
39
addressing the socio-economic implications of COVID-19 (Hepburn et al. 2020). Governments may be
40
in a stronger position to do so due to their pivotal role in assuring the survival of many businesses during
41
the pandemics. Given the magnitude of recovery packages and their implications (Pollitt et al. 2021),
42
choosing the direction of recovery packages amounts to choosing a development pathway (Cross-
43
Chapter Box 1).
