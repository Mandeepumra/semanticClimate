Most first NDCs cover all specific sectors, including LULUCF, and communicate specific targets for
11
individual sub-sectors to support their overall mitigation targets. Concrete actions and priority areas are
12
more detailed in the energy sector, with increased share of renewable energies and energy efficiency
13
being highlighted in the majority of NDCs. Given the uncertainty behind LULUCF emission and
14
removal accounting (Grassi et al. 2017; Jian et al. 2019), several countries state that their accounting
15
framework will only be defined in later NDCs. The GHG included and the global warming potentials
16
(GWPs) used to aggregate emissions also vary across NDCs. Most countries only refer to carbon
17

18
few NDCs also include fluorinated gases and use IPCC AR5 GWPs. The shares of Parties that indicate
19

20
both nearly doubled in new or updated NDCs.

There is considerable literature on country-level mitigation pathways, including but not limited to
22
NDCs. Country distribution of this literature is very unequal (robust evidence, high agreement). In
23
particular, there is a growing literature on (I)NDCs, with a wide scope which includes estimate of
24
emissions levels of NDCs (see section 4.2.2.2); alignment with sustainable development goals (Caetano
25
et al. 2020; Campagnolo and Davide 2019; Fuso Nerini et al. 2019; Antwi-Agyei et al. 2018); ambition
26
(Höhne et al. 2018; Vogt-Schilb and Hallegatte 2017; Hermwille et al. 2019); energy development
27
(Scott et al. 2018); and the legality of downgrading NDCs (Rajamani and Brunnée 2017). Other studies
28
note that many NDCs contain single-year mitigation targets, and suggest that a multi-year trajectory is
29
important for more rigorous monitoring (Elliott et al. 2017; Dagnet et al. 2017).
30
The literature also points out that beyond the ‘headline numbers’, information in (I)NDCs is difficult to
31
analyse (Pauw et al. 2018). Information for ‘clarity, transparency and understanding’ is to be
32
communicated with NDCs, although initial guidance was not specific (UNFCCC 2014). While the
33
adoption of the Paris rule-book provided some greater specificity (UNFCCC 2018b,c), the information
34
included in the NDCs remains uneven. Many NDCs omit important mitigation sectors and do not
35
adequately provide details on costs and financing of implementation (Pauw et al. 2018). Countries are
36
also invited to explain how their NDCs are fair and ambitious, though the way this has been done so far
37
has been criticised as insufficiently rigorous (Winkler et al. 2018).
