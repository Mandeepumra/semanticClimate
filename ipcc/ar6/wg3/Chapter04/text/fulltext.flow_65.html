4.3.1.4
An approach of SDPS helps manage trade-offs between mitigation and other SDGs.
3
Beyond removing structural obstacles to accelerated mitigation, broadening the approach to policies
4
that facilitate shifts in development pathways also helps manage the potential tradeoffs between
5
mitigation and other development objectives discussed in section 4.2.7.
6
Systematic studies of the 17 SDGs have found the interactions among them to be manifold and complex
7
(Pradhan et al. 2017; Nilsson et al. 2016; Weitz et al. 2018; Fuso Nerini et al. 2019). Addressing them
8
calls for interventions affecting fundamental, interconnected, structural features of global society
9
(International Panel on Social Progress 2018; TWI2050 - The World in 2050 2018), such as to our
10
physical infrastructure (e.g., energy, water, industrial, urban infrastructure) (Thacker et al. 2019;
11
Adshead et al. 2019; Waage et al. 2015; Mansell et al. 2019; Chester 2019), our societal institutions
12
(e.g., educational, public health, economic, innovation, and political institutions) (Sachs et al. 2019;
13
Ostrom 2010; Kläy et al. 2015; Messner 2015), and behavioural and cultural tendencies (e.g.,
14
consumption patterns, conventional biases, discriminatory interpersonal and intergroup dynamics, and
15
inequitable power structures) (Esquivel 2016; Sachs et al. 2019). These observations imply that attempt
16
to address each SDG in isolation, or as independent technical challenges, would be insufficient, as
17
would incremental, marginal changes. In contrast, effectively addressing the SDGs is likely to mean
18
significant disruption of long-standing trends and transformative progress to shift development
19
pathways to meet all the SDGs, including climate action, beyond incremental changes targeted at
20
addressing mitigation objectives in isolation. In other words, mitigation conceived as incremental
21
change is not enough. Transformational change has implications for equity in its multiple dimensions
22
(Leach et al. 2018; Klinsky et al. 2017a; Steffen and Stafford Smith 2013) including just transitions
23
(4.5).
24
Working Group II examines climate resilient development pathways – continuous processes that imply
25
deep societal changes and/or transformation, so as to strengthen sustainable development, efforts to
26
eradicate poverty and reduce inequalities while promoting fair and cross-scalar capacities for adaptation
27
to global warming and reduction of GHG emissions in the atmosphere. Transformative action in the
28
context of CRD specifically concerns leveraging change in the five dimensions of development (people,
29
prosperity, partnership, peace, planet) (WGII chapter 18).

4.3.2
Implications of development pathways for mitigation and mitigative capacity 35
4.3.2.1
Countries have different development priorities
36
At the global level, the SDGs adopted by all the United Nations Member States in 2015 are delineated
37
with a view to end poverty, protect the planet and ensure that all people enjoy peace and prosperity by
38
2030. The 17 SDGs are integrated and imply that development must balance social, economic and
39
environmental sustainability.
40
While all countries share the totality of the SDGs, development priorities differ across countries and
41
over time. These priorities are strongly linked to local contexts, and depend on which dimensions of
42
improvements in the well-being of people are considered the most urgent.
