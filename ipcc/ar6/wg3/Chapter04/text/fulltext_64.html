4.3.1.3
Expanding the range of policies and other mitigative options
5
Shifting development pathways aims to influence the ultimate drivers of emissions (and development
6
generally), such as the systemic and cultural determinants of consumption patterns, the political systems
7
and power structures that govern decision making, the institutions and incentives that guide and
8
constrain socio-technical innovation, and the norms and information platforms that shape knowledge
9
and discourse, and culture, values and needs (Raskin et al. 2002). These ultimate drivers determine the
10
mitigative capacity of a society.
11
Decision-makers might usefully consider a broader palette of policies and measures as part of an overall
12
strategy to meet climate goals and other sustainable development goals (see 4.3.2; Table 4.12). This is
13
consistent with the fact that mitigation is increasingly understood to be inseparable from broader
14
developmental goals, which can be facilitated by policy coherence and integration with broader
15
objectives and policies sectorally and societally. This is supported by other observations that mitigation
16
measures based on conventional climate policy instruments, such as emissions taxes or permits, price
17

18
