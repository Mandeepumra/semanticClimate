12

A concrete focal point on these issues has been that of “just transition”. Getting broad consensus for the
18
transformational changes entailed in moving from a high- to a low-carbon economy means ‘leaving no
19
one behind’, i.e., ensuring (sufficiently) equitable transition for the relevant affected individuals,
20
workers, communities, sectors, regions and countries (Newell & Mulvaney, 2013; Jasanoff 2018). The
21
concept of a “just transition” owes its origin to the US trade union movement of the 1980s. The earliest
22
version of a just transition was called the “Superfund for Workers” modelled on the 1980 Superfund
23
program that designed federal funds for the clean-up of toxic substances from chemicals, mining and
24
energy production (Stevis and Felli 2015). It was further taken up, for example in the collaboration of
25
the International Trade Union Confederation (ITUC), the International Labour Organization (ILO) and
26
the UN Environmental Programme (UNEP) in promoting “green jobs” as integral elements of a just
27
transition (ILO 2015; Rosemberg 2015). In recent years the concept of a “just transition” has gained
28
increased traction, for example incorporated in the outcome of the Rio+20 Earth Summit and more
29
recently recognised in the preamble of the Paris Agreement, which states “the imperative of a just
30
transition of the workforce and the creation of decent work and quality jobs in accordance with
31
nationally defined development priorities” (UNFCCC 2015c). Some heads of state and government
32
signed a Solidarity and Just Transition Silesia Declaration first introduced at COP24 in Poland (HoSG
33
2018).

35

36
