Prioritising is one way to manage trade-offs, addressing some national development objectives earlier
21
than others. Another way is to adopt policy packages aimed at shifting development pathways towards
22
sustainability as they expand the range of tools available to simultaneously achieve multiple
23
development objectives, including mitigation. In the city example of section 2a, a carbon tax alone
24
would run counter to other development objectives if it made suburban households locked into high
25
emissions transport modes poorer or if it restricted mobility choices, in particular for low- and middle-
26
income households. Policy packages combining affordable housing and provision of safe low-carbon
27
mobility could both facilitate equitable access to housing (a major development objective in many
28
countries) and make it easier to mitigate by shifting the urban development pathway.

31

32

33
achievement of associated SDGs. Some studies assume Western European lifestyle as a reference for
34
the global North and an improvement in the living standard for the Global South to reduce energy
35

36
global North to achieve a decent living standard for all (Millward-Hopkins et al. 2020; Bertram et al.
37

38

39

3. What does shifting development pathways towards sustainability entail?
42
Shifting development pathways towards sustainability implies making transformative changes that
43
disrupt existing developmental trends. Such choices would not be marginal (Stern and Professor 2009),
44
but include technology adoption, infrastructure availability and use, and socio-behavioural factors
45
(Chapter 5).
