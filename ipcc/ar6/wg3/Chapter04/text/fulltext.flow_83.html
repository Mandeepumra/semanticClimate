4.4.1.4
Channelling financial resources
14
Accelerated mitigation and shifting development pathways necessitate both re-directing existing
15
financial flows from high- to low-emissions technologies and systems and providing additional
16
resources (robust evidence, high agreement). An example is changes in investments from fossil fuels to
17
renewable energy, with pressures to disinvest in the former while increasing levels of ‘green finance’
18
(6.7.4, 15.5). While some lower-carbon technologies have become competitive (1.4.3, 2.5), support
19
remains needed for the low-emissions options have higher costs per unit of service provided than high-
20
emission ones. Lack of financial resources is identified as a major barrier to the implementation of
21
accelerated mitigation and of shifts in development pathways. Overcoming this obstacle has two major
22
components. One relates to private capital. The other to public finance.
23
There is substantial amount of research on the redirection of private financial flows towards low-carbon
24
investment and the role of financial regulators and central banks, as detailed in Chapter 15. Financial
25
systems are an indispensable element of a systemic transition (Fankhauser et al. 2016; Naidoo 2020).
26
Policy frameworks can re-direct financial resources towards low-emission assets and services (UNEP
27
2015), mainstreaming climate finance within financial and banking system regulation, and reducing
28
transaction costs for bankable mitigation technology projects (Mundaca et al. 2013; Brunner and Enting
29
2014; Yeo 2019). Shifts in the financial system to finance climate mitigation and other SDGs can be
30
achieved by aligning incentives and investments with multiple objectives (UNEP Inquiry 2016).
31
Different approaches have been explored to improve such alignment (15.6), from national credit
32
policies to directly green mainstream financial regulations (e.g., through modifications in the Basel
33
rules for banks). For all approaches, an essential precondition is to assess and monitor the contribution
34
of financial flows to climate and sustainability goals, with better metrics that clearly link with financial
35
activity (Chenet et al. 2019). Enabling the alignment of investment decision-making with achieving
36
climate and broader sustainability goals includes acknowledgment and disclosure of climate-change
37
related risk and of risks associated with mitigation in financial portfolios. Current disclosures remain
38
far from the scale the markets need to channel investment to sustainable and resilient solutions (UNEP
39
- Finance Initiative 2020; Clark et al. 2018; Task Force on Climate-Related Financial Disclosures 2019;
40
IPCC 2018b). Disclosure, however, is not enough (Ameli et al. 2020). In addition, climate targets can
41
be translated into investment roadmaps and financing needs for financial institutions, both at national
42
and international level. Financing needs are usable for financial institutions, to inform portfolio
43
allocation decisions and financing priorities (Chenet et al. 2019). At the international level, for example,
44
technology roadmaps for key sectors can be translated into investment roadmaps and financing needs,
45
as shown by existing experiences in energy and industrial sectors (Chenet et al. 2019; WBSCD 2018;
46
International Energy Agency 2015)
