Development priorities are reflected in the decisions that actors within societies make, such as policy
1
choices by governments and parliaments at all levels, votes over competing policy platforms by citizens,
2
or selection of issues that non-state actors push for. Multiple objectives range from poverty eradication
3
to providing energy access, addressing concerns of inequality, providing education, improving health,
4
cleaning air and water, improving connectivity, sustaining growth and providing jobs, among others.
5
For example, eradicating poverty and reducing inequality is a key development priority across many
6
countries, such as Brazil (Grottera et al. 2017), Indonesia (Irfany and Klasen 2017), India (GoI, 2015),
7
South Africa (Winkler 2018) and other low- and middle-income countries (Dorband et al. 2019).
8
Reducing inequality relates not only to income, but also to other dimensions such as in access to energy
9
services (Tait 2017), gender, education, racial and ethnic profiles (Andrijevic et al. 2020), and thereby
10
assumes relevance in both developing and developed countries. The development priorities of many
11
poor countries and communities with low capacities to adapt, has been focused more on reducing
12
poverty, providing basic infrastructure, education and improving health, rather than on mitigation
13
(Chimhowu et al. 2019).

4.3.2.2
The nature of national development plans is changing
15
Governments are increasingly resorting to the development of national plans to build institutions,
16
resources, and risk/shock management capabilities to guide national development. The number of
17
countries with a national development plan has more than doubled, from about 62 in 2006 (World Bank
18
2007) to 134 plans published between 2012 and 2018 (Chimhowu et al. 2019). The comeback of
19
planning may be linked to increased consideration given to sustainability, which is by construction
20
forward-looking and far ranging, and therefore requires state and civil society to prepare and implement
21
plans at all levels of governance. Governments are increasingly engaging in the development and
22
formulation of national plans in an organised, conscious and continual attempt to select the best
23
available alternatives to achieve specific goals.
24
A systematic assessment of 107 national development plans and 10 country case studies provides useful
25
insights regarding the type and content of the plans (Chimhowu et al. 2019). development plans are
26
increasingly focusing on mobilising action across multiple actors and multiple dimensions to enhance
27
resilience and improve the ability to undertake stronger mitigation actions. Various initiatives such as
28
the World Summit for Children in 1990; the Heavily Indebted Poor Country initiative that started
29
offering debt relief in exchange for commitments by beneficiary states to invest in health, education,
30
nutrition and poverty reduction in 1996; and push towards Comprehensive Development Frameworks
31
seem to have catalysed the development of national actions plans across countries to estimate, measure
32
and track investments and progress towards SDGs.
33
The most recent development plans also tend to differ from the earlier ones in terms of their approach.
34
Complexity science has over the years argued for new forms of planning based on contingency,
35
behaviour change, adaptation and constant learning (Colander and Kupers 2016; Ramalingam, 2013),
36
and new plans have increasingly focused on increasing resilience of individuals, organisations and
37
systems (Hummelbrunner and Jones, 2013). Finally, alongside short-term (typically 5 year) plans with
38
operational purpose, countries have also expressed visions of their development pathways over longer
39
time horizons, via, e.g., Voluntary National Reviews submitted in the context of the UN High Level
40
Political Forum on Sustainable Development.
41
National development plans are also increasingly more holistic in their approach, linking closely with
42
SDGs and incorporating climate action in their agendas. For instance, the Low Carbon Development
43
Initiative (LCDI), launched in 2017 by the Government of Indonesia, seeks to identify the development
44
policies that can help Indonesia achieve multiple (social, economic, and environmental) goals
45
simultaneously along with preserving and improving the country’s natural resources (Bappenas 2019).
