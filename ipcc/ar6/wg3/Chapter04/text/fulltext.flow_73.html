Policies such as those listed in Table 4.12 are typically associated with broader objectives than GHG
3
mitigation. They are generally conceived and implemented in the pursuit of overall societal
4
development objectives, such as job creation, macro-economic stability, economic growth, and public
5
health and welfare. However, they can have major influence on mitigative capacity, and hence can be
6
seen as necessary tools if mitigation options are to be significantly broadened and accelerated (medium
7
evidence, medium agreement). The example of the UK shows how accelerated mitigation through
8
dietary changes require a wide set of efforts to shift underlying drivers of behaviour. In this case,
9
multiple forces have interacted to lead to reduced meat consumption, including health attitudes, animal
10
welfare concerns, and an increasing focus on climate and other environmental impacts of livestock
11
production, along with corporate investment in market opportunities, and technological developments
12
in meat alternatives (Box 5.5).
