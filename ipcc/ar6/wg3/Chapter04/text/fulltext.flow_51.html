4.2.6.2
Mitigation and economic growth in the near- and mid-term
23
A significant part of the literature assesses the impacts of mitigation on GDP, consistent with
24
policymakers’ interest in this variable. It must be noted upfront that computable equilibrium models,
25
on which our assessments are mostly based, capture the impact of mitigation on GDP and other core
26
economic variables while typically overlooking other effects that may matter (like improvements in air
27
quality). Second, even though GDP (or better, GDP per capita) is not an indicator of welfare (Fleurbaey
28
and Blanchet 2013), changes in GDP per capita across countries and over time are highly correlated
29
with changes in welfare indicators in the areas of poverty, health, and education (Gable et al. 2015).
30
The mechanisms linking mitigation to GDP outlined below would remain valid even with alternative
31
indicators of well-being (5.2.1). Third, another stream of literature criticises the pursuit of economic
32
growth as a goal, instead advocating a range of alternatives and suggesting modelling of post-growth
33
approaches to achieve rapid mitigation while improving social outcomes (Hickel et al. 2021). In the
34
language of the present chapter, these alternatives constitute alternative development pathways.
35

36

37
