A second set of likely obstacles in the short-term to accelerated mitigation revolves around undesirable
2
distributional consequences, within and across countries. As discussed in 4.2.6.3, the distributional
3
implications of climate policies depend strongly on their design, the way they are implemented, and on
4
the context into which they are inserted. Distributional implications of climate policies have both ethics
5
and equity dimensions, to determine what is desirable/acceptable by a given society in a given context,
6
notably the relative power of different winners and losers to have their interests taken into account, or
7
not, in the relevant decision-making processes. Like costs, distributional implications of accelerated
8
mitigation are rooted in the underlying socio-political-institutional structures of a society.
9
A third set of obstacles are about technology availability and adoption. Lack of access even to existing
10
cost-effective mitigation technologies remains an important issue, particularly for many developing
11
countries, and even in the short-term. Though it relates most directly to techno-economic costs,
12
technology availability raises broader issues related to the sociotechnical systems within which
13
innovation and adoption are embedded, and issues of technology availability are inherently issues of
14
systemic failure (16.3). The underlying legal, economic and social structures of the economy are central
15
to the different stages of socio-transition processes (Cross-Chapter Box 12).
16
The last set of obstacles revolves around the unsuitability of existing structures to accelerated
17
mitigation. We include here all forms of established structures, material (e.g., physical capital) or not
18
(institutions, social norms, patterns of individual behaviour), that are potentially long-lived and limit
19
the implementation of accelerated mitigation pathways. Typically, such structures exist for reasons
20
other than climate change and climate mitigation, including the distribution of power among various
21
actors. Modifying them in the name of accelerated climate mitigation thus requires to deal with other
22
non-climate issues as well. For example, resolving the landlord-tenant dilemma, an institutional barrier
23
to the deployment of energy efficiency in building, opens fundamental questions on private property in
24
buildings.
