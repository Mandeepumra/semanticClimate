Box 4.5 South Africa’s National Development Plan
11
South Africa adopted its first National Development Plan (NDP) in 2011 (NPC 2011), the same year in
12
which the country adopted climate policy (RSA 2011) and hosted COP17 in Durban. Chapter 5 of the
13
NDP addresses environmental sustainability in the context of development planning, and specifically
14
“an equitable transition to a low-carbon economy” (NPC 2011). The chapter refers explicitly to the need
15
for a just transition, protecting the poor from impacts and any transitional costs from emissions-
16
intensive to low-carbon. The plan proposes several mitigation measures, including a carbon budgeting
17
approach, reference to Treasury’s carbon tax, use of various low carbon options while maintaining
18
energy security, and the integrated resource plan for electricity. The NDP refers to coal in several
19
chapters, in some places suggesting additional investment (including new rail lines to transport coal and
20
coal to liquids), in others decommissioning coal-fired power “Procuring at least 20 000MW of
21
renewable electricity by 2030, importing electricity from the region, decommissioning 11 000MW of
22
ageing coal-fired power stations and stepping up investments in energy-efficiency” (NPC 2011: p.46).
23
Reference to environmental sustainability is not limited to chapter 5 – the introductory vision statement
24
includes acknowledgement “that each and every one of us is intimately and inextricably of this earth
25
with its beauty and life-giving sources; that our lives on earth are both enriched and complicated by
26
what we have contributed to its condition” (NPC 2011: p. 21); and the overview of the plan includes a
27
section on climate change, addressing both mitigation and adaptation.

Looking ahead, given that different development pathways can lead to different levels of GHG
31
emissions and to different capacities and opportunities to mitigate, there is increasing research on how
32
to make development pathways more sustainable. Literature is also focusing on the need for a “new
33
normal” as a system capable of achieving higher quality growth while addressing multiple development
34
objectives by focusing on “innovative development pathways”.
35
Literature suggests that if development pathways are to be changed to address the climate change
36
problem, choices that would need to be made about development pathways would not be marginal
37
(Stern and Professor 2009), and would require a new social contract to address a complex set of inter-
38
linkages across sectors, classes and the whole economy (Winkler 2017b). Shifting development
39
pathways necessitates planning in a holistic manner, rather than thinking about discrete and isolated
40
activities and actions to undertake mitigation. Further, the necessary transformational changes can be
41
positive if they are rooted in the development aspirations of the economy and society in which they take
42
place (Dubash 2012; Jones et al. 2013), but they can also lead to carbon colonialism if the
43
transformations are imposed by Northern donors or perceived as such.
