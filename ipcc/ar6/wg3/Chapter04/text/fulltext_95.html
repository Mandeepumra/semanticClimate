Farming system approaches to agricultural mitigation have a wide variety of co-benefits and tradeoffs.
6
Indeed, there are conceptual formulations for these practices in which the co-benefits are more of a
7
focus, such as climate-smart agriculture (CSA) which ties mitigation to adaptation through its three
8
pillars of increased productivity, mitigation, and adaptation (Lipper et al. 2014). The ‘4 per 1000’ goal
9
to increase soil carbon by 0.4% per year (Soussana et al. 2019) is compatible with the three pillars of
10
CSA. Sustainable intensification, a framework which centers around a need for increased agricultural
11
production within environmental constraints also complements CSA (Campbell et al. 2014). The
12
literature reports examples of mitigation co-benefits of adaptation actions, with evidence from various
13
regions (Chapter 7, Thornton and Herrero (2015), Thornton et al. (2018)).
14
Conservation agriculture, promoted for improving agricultural soils and crop diversity (Powlson et al.
15
2016) can help build adaptive capacity (Smith et al. 2017; Pradhan et al. 2018a) and yield mitigation
16
co-benefits through improved fertiliser use or efficient use of machinery and fossil fuels (Cui et al.
17
2018; Harvey et al. 2014; Pradhan et al. 2018a).
18
There is a complex set of barriers to implementation of farming-system approaches for climate
19
mitigation (7.6.4), suggesting a need for deliberate shifts in development pathways to achieve
20
significant progress in this sector. The link between NDCs and mitigation in the land use sector can
21
provide impetus for such policies. For example, there are multiple agricultural mitigation options that
22
southeast Asian countries could use to meet NDCs that would have an important adaptive impact
23
(Amjath-Babu et al. 2019).
24
Some agricultural practices considered sustainable have trade-offs, and their implementation can have
25
negative effects on adaptation or other ecosystem services. Fast-growing tree monocultures or biofuel
26
crops may enhance carbon stocks but reduce downstream water availability and decrease availability of
27
agricultural land (Windham-Myers et al. 2018; Kuwae and Hori 2019). In some dry environments
28
similarly, agroforestry can increase competition with crops and pastures, decreasing productivity, and
29
reduce catchment water yield (Schrobback et al. 2011).
30
Agricultural practices can adapt to climate change while decreasing CO
2
emissions on the farm field.
31
However, if such a practice leads to lower yields, interconnections of the global agricultural system can
32
lead to land use change elsewhere and a net increase in GHG emissions (Erb et al. 2016).
33
Implementation of sustainable agriculture can increase or decrease yields depending on context (Pretty
34
et al. 2006).

Blue carbon and mitigation co-benefits of adaptation actions
36
The Paris Agreement recognises that mitigation co-benefits resulting from Parties’ adaptation actions
37
and/or economic diversification plans can contribute to mitigation outcomes (UNFCCC 2015: Article
38
4.7). Blue carbon refers to biologically-driven carbon flux or storage in coastal ecosystems such as
39
seagrasses, salt marshes, and mangroves (Wylie et al. 2016; Fennessy et al. 2019; Fourqurean et al.
40
2012; Tokoro et al. 2014) (see Cross-Chapter Box 8 on blue carbon as a storage medium and removal
41
process).
