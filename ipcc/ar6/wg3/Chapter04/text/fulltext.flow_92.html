4.4.2
Adaptation, development pathways and mitigation 23
Mitigation actions are strongly linked to adaptation. These connections come about because mitigation
24
actions can be adaptive (e.g., some agroforestry projects) but also through policy choices (e.g., climate
25
finance is allocated among adaptation or mitigation projects) and even biophysical links (e.g., climate
26
trajectories, themselves determined by mitigation, can influence the viability of adaptation projects).
27
As development pathways shape the levers and enablers available to a society (4.3.1, Figure 4.7), a
28
broader set of enabling conditions also helps with adaptation (medium evidence, high agreement).
29
Previous assessments have consistently recognised this linkage. The Paris Agreement includes
30
mitigation and adaptation as key areas of action, through NDCs and communicating adaptation actions
31
and plans. The Agreement explicitly recognises that mitigation co-benefits resulting from adaptation
32
can count towards NDC targets. The IPCC Fifth Assessment Report (IPCC 2014) emphasised that
33
sustainable development is helpful in going beyond a narrow focus on separate mitigation and
34
adaptation options and their specific co-benefits. The IPCC Special Report on climate change and land
35
addresses GHG emissions from land-based ecosystems with a focus on the vulnerability of land-based
36
systems to climate change. The report identifies the potential of changes to land use and land
37
management practices to mitigate and adapt to climate change, and to generate co-benefits that help
38
meet other SDGs (Jian et al. 2019).
39
