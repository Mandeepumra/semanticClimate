4.2.5.11
Lowering demand, downscaling economies
16
Studies have identified socio-technological pathways to help achieve net zero CO
2
and GHG targets at
17
national scale, that in aggregate are crucial to keeping global temperature below agreed limits. However,
18
most of the literature focuses on supply-side options, including carbon dioxide removal mechanisms
19
(BECCS, afforestation, and others) that are not fully commercialised (Cross-Chapter Box 8). Costs to
20
research, deploy, and scale up these technologies are often high. Recent studies have addressed lowering
21
demand through energy conversion efficiency improvements, but few studies have considered demand
22
reduction through efficiency (Grubler et al. 2018) and the related supply implications and mitigation
23
measures.
24
Five main drivers of long-term energy demand reduction that can meet the 1.5˚C target include quality
25
of life, urbanisation, novel energy services, diversification of end-user roles, and information innovation
26
(Grubler et al. 2018). A low-energy-demand scenario requires fundamental societal and institutional
27
transformation from current patterns of consumption, including: decentralised services and increased
28
granularity (small-scale, low-cost technologies to provide decentralised services), increased use value
29
from services (multi-use vs. single use), sharing economies, digitalisation, and rapid transformation
30
driven by end-user demand. This approach to transformation differs from the status quo and current
31
climate change policies in emphasising energy end-use and services first, with downstream effects
32
driving intermediate and upstream structural change.
33
Radical low carbon innovation involves systemic, cultural, and policy changes and acceptance of
34
uncertainty in the beginning stages. However, the current dominant analytical perspectives are grounded
35
in neoclassical economics and social psychology, and focus primarily on marginal changes rather than
36
radical transformations (Geels et al. 2018). Some literature is beginning to focus on mitigation through
37
behaviour and lifestyle changes, but specific policy measures for supporting such changes and their
38
contribution to emission reductions remain unclear (see also Section 4.4.2 and Chapter 5).
