In sum, development pathways unfold over time in response to complex dynamics among various
11
drivers and diverse actors with varying interests and motivations (high agreement, robust evidence).
12
The way countries develop determines the nature and degree of the obstacles to accelerating mitigation
13
and achieving other sustainable development objectives (medium-robust evidence, medium agreement).
14
Meeting ambitious mitigation and development goals cannot be achieved through incremental change
15
(robust evidence, medium agreement). Shifting development pathways thus involves designing and
16
implementing policies where possible to intentionally enhance enabling conditions and reduce obstacles
17
to desired outcomes (medium evidence, medium agreement).

22

What have we learned so far? As highlighted above, despite 30 years of UNFCCC and growing
30
contributions by non-state actors, the emissions gap keeps growing (4.2.2 and 4.2.3). Mitigation
31
conceived as incremental change is not enough. Meeting ambitious mitigation goals entails rapid, non-
32
marginal changes in production and consumption patterns (4.2.4 and 4.2.5). Taking another approach,
33
we have seen in section 4.3 that shifting development pathways broadens the scope for mitigation (4.3.1,
34
4.3.2) and offers more opportunities than mitigation alone to combine mitigation with the realisation of
35
other SDGs (4.3.1, Cross-Chapter Box 5).
36
A practical way forward is to combine shifting development pathways and accelerating mitigation
37
(medium evidence, high agreement). This means introducing multi-objective policy packages and
38
sequences with climate and development components that both target mitigation directly and create the
39
conditions for shifts in development pathways that will help accelerate further mitigation down the line,
40
and meet other development objectives. Since development pathways result from a myriad of decisions
41
from multiple actors (4.3.1), coordination across countries and with non-state actors is essential.
