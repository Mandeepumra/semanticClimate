4.2.5.2
Accelerated decarbonisation of electricity through renewable energy
12
Power generation could decarbonise much faster with scaled up deployment of renewable energy and
13
storage. Both technologies are mature, available, and fast decreasing in costs, more than for many other
14
mitigation options. Models continuously underestimate the speed at which renewables and storage
15
expand. Higher penetration of renewable energy in the power sector is a common theme in scenarios.
16
Some studies provide cost optimal electricity mix under emission constraints, while others explicitly
17
explore a 100% renewables or 100% emission free electricity sector (Box 4.3).
18
Figure 4.3 shows an increasing share of renewable electricity in most countries historically, with further
19
increases projected in many decarbonisation pathways. Targets for very high shares of renewable
20
electricity generation—up to 100%—are shown for a number of countries, with the global share
21
projected to range from 60% to 70% for 1.5°C with no overshoot (C0) to below 2°C (C4) scenarios.
22
Countries and States that have set 100% renewables targets include Scotland for 2020 (Scottish
23
Government 2021), Austria (2030), Denmark (2035) and California (2045) (Figure 4.3).
24
While 100% renewable electricity generation by 2050 is found to be feasible, it is not without issues.
25
For example, (Jacobson et al. 2017, 2019) find it feasible for 143 countries with only a 9% average
26
increase in economic costs (considering all social costs) if annual electricity demand can be reduced by
27
57%. Others state that challenges exist with speed of expansion, ensuring sufficient supply at all times
28
or higher costs compared to other alternatives (Clack et al. 2017). In-depth discussion of net zero
29
electricity systems can be found in section 6.6.
