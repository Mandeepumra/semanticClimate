4.4.1.7
Example: Structural change provides a way to keep jobs and mitigate
1
Developing countries have experienced a period of rapid economic growth in the past two decades.
2
Patterns of growth have differed markedly across regions, with newly emerging East Asian economies
3
building on transition to manufacturing—as China has done in the past—while Latin American
4
countries tend to transition directly from primary sector to services (Rodrik 2016), and African countries
5
tend to rely on productivity improvements in the primary sectors (Diao et al. 2019). Yet many countries
6
still face the challenge of getting out of the “middle-income trap” (Agénor and Canuto 2015), as labour-
7
saving technological change and globalisation have limited options to develop via the manufacturing
8
sector (Altenburg and Rodrik 2017).
9
Looking ahead, several studies have illustrated how structural change towards sustainability could lead
10
to reduced emissions intensity and higher mitigative capacity. In China, for example, the shift away
11
from heavy industry (to light industry and services) has already been identified as the most important
12
force limiting emissions growth (Guan et al. 2018), and as a major factor for future emissions (Kwok
13
et al. 2018).
14
Overall, Altenburg et al. (2017) argue that reallocation of capital and labour from low- to high-
15
productivity sectors—i.e., structural change—remains a necessity, and that it is possible to combine it
16
with reduced environmental footprint (including, but not limited to, mitigation). They argue that this
17
dual challenge calls for structural transformation policies different from those implemented in the past,
18
most importantly through a “systematic steering of investment behaviour in a socially agreed direction”
19
and encompassing policy coordination (limited evidence, medium agreement).
20
In order to permit progress on their SDG agendas, it is essential that countries develop visions of their
21
future decarbonised sectoral production structure, including its ability to generate growth in incomes,
22
employment and foreign exchange earnings. as well as the related spatial distribution of production,
23
employment, and housing. To this extent, governance and institutional capacity matter, such as
24
availability of tools to support long-term planning. A sectoral structure that permits strong growth is
25
essential given strong associations between growth in per-capita incomes and progress on most SDGs
26
(including those related to poverty; health; education; and access to water, sanitation, electricity, and
27
roads; but not income equality), in part due to the fact that higher incomes provide both households and
28
governments with resources that at least in part would be used to promote SDGs (Gable et al. 2015).
29
The future viability of sectors will depend on the extent to which they can remain profitable while
30
relying on lower-carbon energy. The challenge to identify alternative sectors of growth is particularly
31
acute for countries that today depend on oil and natural gas for most of their foreign exchange and
32
government revenues (Mirzoev et al. 2020). Changes in economic structure will also have gender
33
implications since the roles of men and women vary across sectors. For example, in many developing
34
countries, sectors in which women play a relatively important role, including agriculture and unpaid
35
household services like collection of water and fuel wood, may be negatively affected by climate change
36
(Roy 2018). It may thus be important to take complementary actions to address the gender implications
37
of changes in economic structure.
38
Given strong complementarities between policies discussed above, an integrated policy approach is
39
crucial. For example, as suggested, the actions that influence the pace at which GHG emissions can be
40
cut with political support may depend on taxation (including carbon taxes), investments in
41
infrastructure, spending on R&D, changes in income distribution (influenced by transfers), and
42
communication. In this light, it is important to consider the demands that alternative policy packages
43
put on government policy-making efficiency and credibility as well as the roles of other enabling
