4.2.7
Obstacles to accelerated mitigation and how overcoming them amounts to shifts 11
in development pathways 12
As outlined in Sections 4.2.3, 4.2.4, 4.2.5 and 4.2.6 there is improved understanding since AR5 of what
13
accelerated mitigation would entail in the coming decades. A major finding is that accelerated
14
mitigation pathways in the near- to mid-term appear technically and economically feasible in most
15
contexts. Chapter 4, however, cannot stop here. Section 4.2.2 has documented an important policy gap
16
for current climate pledges, and Cross-Chapter Box 4 shows an even larger ambition gap between
17
current pledges and what would be needed in the near- term to be on pathways consistent with below
18
2°C, let alone 1.5°C. In other words, while the implementation of mitigation policies to achieve updated
19
NDC almost doubles the mitigation efforts, and notwithstanding the widespread availability of the
20
necessary technologies, this doubling of effort merely narrows the gap to pathways consistent with 2°C
21
by at most 20%.
22
Obstacles to the implementation of accelerated mitigation pathways can be grouped in four main
23
categories (Table 4.10). The first set of arguments can be understood through the lens of cost-benefit
24
analysis of decision-makers, as they revolve around the following question: Are costs too high relative
25
to benefits? More precisely, are the opportunity costs—in economics terms, what is being forfeited by
26
allocating scarce resources to mitigation—justified by the benefits for the decision-maker (whether
27
individual, firm, or nation)? This first set of obstacles is particularly relevant because accelerated
28
mitigation pathways imply significant effort in the short-run, while benefits in terms of limited warming
29
accrue later and almost wholly to other actors. However, as discussed in 3.6 and 4.2.6, mitigation costs
30
for a given mitigation target are not carved in stone. They strongly depend on numerous factors,
31
including the way mitigation policies have been designed, selected, and implemented, the processes
32
through which markets have been shaped by market actors and institutions, and nature of socially- and
33
culturally-determined influences on consumer preferences. Hence, mitigation choices that might be
34
expressed straightforwardly as techno-economic decisions are, at a deeper level, strongly conditioned
35
by underlying structures of society.
