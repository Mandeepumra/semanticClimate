4.4.1.9
Example: Combining housing policies with carbon taxation can deliver both housing and
8
mitigation in the transport sector
9
The spatial distribution of households and firms across urban and rural areas is a central characteristic
10
of development pathways. Patterns of urbanisation, territorial development, and regional integration
11
have wide-ranging implications for economic, social and environmental objectives (World Bank 2009).
12
Notably, choices regarding spatial forms of development have large-scale implications for demand for
13
transportation and associated GHG emissions.
14
Exclusionary mechanisms such as decreasing accessibility and affordability of inner-urban
15
neighbourhoods is a major cause of suburbanisation of low- to middle-income households (e.g.,
16
(Hochstenbach and Musterd 2018). Suburbanisation, in turn, is associated with higher transportation
17
demand (Bento et al. 2005) and higher carbon footprints for households (Jones and Kammen 2014).
18
Similarly, other studies find a significant positive link between housing prices and energy demand
19
(Lampin et al. 2013).
20
Reducing emissions from transport in cities through traditional climate policy instruments (e.g., through
21
a carbon tax) is more difficult when inner-urban neighbourhoods are less accessible and less affordable,
22
because exclusionary mechanisms act as a countervailing force to the rising transportation costs induced
23
by the climate policy, pushing households outwards rather than inwards. Said differently, the costs of
24
mitigating intra-city transportation emissions are higher when inner-urban housing prices are higher
25
(Lampin et al. 2013).
26
This suggests that policies making inner-urban neighbourhoods more accessible and more affordable
27
can open up broader opportunities for suburban households to relocate in the face of increasing
28
transportation costs. This is particularly important for low- and middle-income households, who spend
29
a greater portion of their income on housing and transportation, and are more likely to be locked into
30
locations that are distant from their jobs. Making inner-urban neighbourhoods more accessible and more
31
affordable has the potential to reduce both the social costs—e.g., households feeling helpless in front
32
of rising fuel prices—and the economic costs of mitigation policies—as a lower price of carbon is likely
33
to achieve the same amount of emission reductions since households have more capacities to adjust.
34
Making inner-cities neighbourhoods more accessible and more affordable is a complex endeavour
35
(Benner and Karner 2016). At the same time, it is already a policy objective in its own right in many
36
countries, independent of the climate mitigation motivation, for a range of social, health and economic
37
reasons. Revenues derived from climate policies could provide additional resources to support such
38
programs, as some climate policy already have provisions to use their revenues towards low-income
39
groups (Karner and Marcantonio 2018). The mitigation benefits of keeping inner-cities more accessible
40
and affordable for low- and middle-income households often remains out of, or is only emerging in the
41
debates surrounding the planning of fast-developing cities in many developing countries (Grant 2015;
42
IADB 2012; Khosla and Bhardwaj 2019). Finally, from a political economy perspective, it is also
43
interesting to note that (Bergquist et al. 2020) find higher support for climate policy packages in the
44
U.S. when affordable housing programs are included.
