How can one shift development pathway and accelerate mitigation? The literature does not provide
4
a complete handbook for shifting development pathways and accelerating mitigation. The literature
5
does, however, shed light on some of the underlying dynamics. Shifting development pathways can be
6
necessitated by the existence of pervasive obstacles that prove prohibitive to reaching mitigation and
7
other development objectives (4.2.7). Deliberate measures taken to facilitate the shifting of
8
development pathways and accelerated mitigation involve putting in place key enabling conditions that
9
help overcome those obstacles (see Figure 4.6)—improving governance and institutional capacity,
10
fostering behavioural change and technological innovation, designing and implementing adequate
11
policy, and finance. Just transitions, while they will differ by context, are critical to identifying and
12
avoiding or addressing inequitable distributive consequences (robust evidence, high agreement).
13
Enabling conditions necessary to accelerate mitigation and shift development pathways are discussed
14
in depth in Chapters 5, 13, 14, 15 and 16. In addition, Chapters 13 and 14 detail the policy instruments
15
that could help shift development pathways and accelerate the scale and pace of mitigation, while
16
Chapter 4 describes those in broad strategies terms. Chapter 13 adds more texture on institutional and
17
governance machinery; policy choice, design and implementation; as well as policy formulation
18
processes, actors and structure across scales.

Since development pathways and mitigation options depend to large extent on national objectives and
20
circumstances, this chapter is primarily concerned with literature at national level (or in the case of the
21
European Union, at regional level), while Chapter 3 is primarily concerned with literature at global
22
scale. The national scale selected in this Chapter requires attention as national mitigation pathways
23
cannot be linked directly to global mitigation goals (see Box 4.2). This chapter is also concerned mostly
24
with economy-wide development and mitigation pathways, as distinct from detailed sectoral work that
25
is assessed in the systems chapters 6 to 12. The present chapter also assesses literature on non-state
26
action.

Chapter 4 draws on five major strands of literature: (1) an emerging literature on development
28
pathways—conceptual, empirical, and model-based, including at the national and sub-national scales;
29
(2) a rapidly expanding, model-based, literature on mitigation pathways in the near- and mid-term
30
(Lepault and Lecocq 2021); (3) studies of NDCs and mid-century strategies; (4) a broader literature on
31
transformation and shifts in development pathways, including from non-climate literatures; and (5) a
32
significant literature on equity, including just transitions. This is supported by a database of country-
33
level mitigation scenarios at country level assembled for the preparation of this Chapter (Annex III,
34
Table I.10 and I.11).

The Chapter builds on past IPCC reports. In AR5, all mitigation pathways were assessed in a single
36
chapter (Clarke et al. 2014), which focused mostly on the long-term. SR1.5 included a chapter on
37
mitigation pathways compatible with the temperature goal in the Paris Agreement (Rogelj et al. 2018a),
38
mostly at the global level. It also considered strengthening mitigation (de Coninck et al. 2018) in the
39
context of poverty, inequality and sustainable development (Roy et al. 2018). Development pathways
40
have also been explored, albeit less frequently, in past IPCC reports starting with the Special Report on
41
Emissions Scenarios (Nakicenovic et al. 2000). Some early framing of development pathways was
42
included in the Third Assessment Report (William R. Moomaw et al. 2001), further developed in the
43
Fourth Assessment Report (Sathaye et al. 2007). An extended discussion of climate change and equity
44
was conducted in AR5 (Fleurbaey et al. 2014).
