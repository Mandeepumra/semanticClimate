4.4.1.3
Governance and institutional capacity
9
Governance for climate mitigation and shifting development pathways is enhanced when tailored to
10
national and local contexts. Improved institutions and governance enable ambitious climate action and
11
help bridge implementation gaps (medium evidence, high agreement). Improving institutions involve a
12
broad range of stakeholders and multiple regional and temporal scales. It necessitates a credible and
13
trusted process for reconciling perspectives and balancing potential side-effects, managing winners and
14
losers and adopting compensatory measures to ensure an inclusive and just transition (Newell and
15
Mulvaney 2013; Miller and Richter 2014; Diffenbaugh and Burke 2019; Gambhir et al. 2018),
16
managing the risk of inequitable or non-representative power dynamics and avoiding regulatory capture
17
by special interests (Helsinki Design Lab 2014; Kahane 2019; Boulle et al. 2015).
18
Long experience of political management of change demonstrates that managing such risks is not easy,
19
and requires sufficiently strong and competent institutions (Stiglitz 1998). For example, shift away
20
from fossil fuel-based energy economy could significantly disrupt the status quo, leading to a stranding
21
of financial and capital assets and shifting of political-economic power. Ensuring the decision-making
22
process is not unduly influenced by actors with much to lose is key to managing a transformation.
23
Effective governance, as noted in Chapter 13, requires establishing strategic direction, coordination of
24
policy responses, and mediation among divergent interests. Among varieties of climate governance,
25
which institutions emerge is path-dependent, based on the interplay of national political institutions,
26
international drivers, and bureaucratic structures (Dubash 2021). Focused national climate institutions
27
to address these challenges are more likely to emerge, persist and be effective when they are consistent
28
with a framing of climate change that has broad national political support (medium evidence, medium
29
agreement) (4.5, 13.2, 13.5).
30
Innovative governance approaches can help meet these challenges (Clark et al. 2018; Díaz et al. 2019).
31
Enabling multilevel governance—i.e., better alignment across governance scales—and coordination of
32
international organisations and national governments can help accelerate a transition to sustainable
33
development and deep decarbonisation (Tait and Euston-Brown 2017; Michaelowa and Michaelowa
34
2017; Ringel 2017; Revi 2017; Cheshmehzangi 2016; IPCC 2018a). Participatory and inclusive
35
governance—partnerships between state and non-state actors—, and concerted effort across different
36
stakeholders are crucial in supporting acceleration (Roberts 2016; Hering et al. 2014; Figueres et al.
37
2017; Leal Filho et al. 2018; Burch et al. 2014; Lee et al. 2018; Clark et al. 2018). So do partnerships
38
through transnational climate governance initiatives, which coordinate nation-states and non-state
39
actors on an international scale (Hsu et al. 2018). Although they are unlikely to close the gap of the
40
insufficient mitigation effort of national governments (Michaelowa and Michaelowa 2017) (4.2.3), they
41
help building confidence in governments concerning climate policy and push for more ambitious
42
national goals (UNEP 2018b).
43
