14

15

A growing literature assesses co-benefits of sectoral policies that lead to decarbonisation and
23
simultaneously promote economic development, improve living standards, reduce inequality, and create
24
job opportunities (Bataille et al. 2018; Pye et al. 2016; Maroun and Schaeffer 2012; Richter et al. 2018;
25
Bataille et al. 2016b; La Rovere et al. 2018; Waisman et al. 2019). While this may be particularly
26
challenging in developing countries, given large populations still lacking basic needs, previous
27
development paths show that finding synergies in development and climate objectives in the AFOLU
28
sector is possible. One example is Brazil, which has arguably shifted its development pathway to reduce
29
emissions and make progress towards several SDGs, though progress is not linear. Over the past two
30
decades, Brazil had made remarkable progress in implementing a sequence of policies across multiple
31
sectors. This policy package simultaneously increased minimum wages of low income families,
32
achieved universal energy access, and raised the quality of life and well-being for the large majority of
33
the population (Da Silveira Bezerra et al. 2017; Grottera et al. 2018, 2017; La Rovere et al. 2018). This
34
led to significant social benefits, reduction of income inequality and poverty eradication (Da Silveira
35
Bezerra et al. 2017; Grottera et al. 2017), reflected in a decrease of the Gini coefficient and a rise in the
36
human development index (La Rovere 2017).
