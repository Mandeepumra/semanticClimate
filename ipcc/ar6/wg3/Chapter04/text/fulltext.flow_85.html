20

Climate actions are related to knowledge but even strongly to motivational factors (Hornsey et al. 2016;
24
Bolderdijk et al. 2013; Boomsma and Steg 2014), which explains the gap between awareness and action
25
(Ünal et al. 2018). Social influences, particularly from peers, affect people’s engagement in climate
26
action (Schelly 2014). Role models appear to have a solid basis in people’s everyday preferences
27
(WBGU 2011). Social norms can reinforce individuals’ underlying motivations and be effective in
28
encouraging sustainable consumption patterns, as many examples offered by behavioural science
29
illustrate. Social networks also influence and spread behaviours (Service et al. 2014; Clayton et al. 2015;
30
Farrow et al. 2017; Shah et al. 2019). These social influences can be harnessed by climate policy.
