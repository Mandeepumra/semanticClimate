Shifting development pathways entails fundamental changes in energy, urban, building, industrial,
8
transport, and land-based systems. It also requires changes in behaviour and social practices.

4. How to shift development pathways?
17
Shifting development paths is complex. If history is any guide, practices that can easily supplant existing
18
systems and are clearly profitable move fastest (Griliches 1957). Changes that involve ‘dissimilar,
19
unfamiliar and more complex science-based components’ take more time, acceptance and legitimation
20
and involve complex social learning (Conley and Udry 2010), even when they promise large gains
21
(Pezzoni et al. 2019).
22
23
Yet despite the complexities of the interactions that result in patterns of development, history also shows
24
that societies can influence the direction of development pathways based on choices made by decision-
25
makers, citizens, the private sector and social stakeholders. For example, fundamentally different
26
responses to the first oil shock shifted then-comparable economies on to different energy sector
27
development and economic pathways in the 1970s and 80s (Sathaye et al. 2009). More recent examples
28
have shown evidence of voluntary transitions for e.g., advanced lighting in Sweden, improved cook-
29
stoves in China, liquefied petroleum gas stoves in Indonesia or ethanol vehicles in Brazil (Sovacool
30
2016).
31
32
There is no one-size-fits-all recipe for shifting development pathways. However, the following insights
33
can be drawn from past experience and scenarios of possible future development pathways (4.4.1).
For
34
example, policies making inner-urban neighbourhoods more accessible and affordable reduce transport
35
costs for low- and middle-income households, and also reduce transport emissions (4.4.1.9). Shifts in
36
development pathways result from both sustained political interventions and bottom-up changes in
37
public opinion. No single sector or policy action is enough to achieve this. Coordinated policy mixes
38
would need to coordinate multiple actors – i.e., individuals, groups and collectives, corporate actors,
39
institutions and infrastructure actors – to deepen decarbonisation and shift pathways towards
40
sustainability (Pettifor 2020). One example was the LPG Subsidy ("Zero Kero") Program in Indonesia
41
which harnessed creative policy design to shift to cleaner energy by overcoming existing private
42
interests. The objective of decreasing fiscal expenditures on domestic kerosene subsidies by replacing
43
it with LPG was achieved by harnessing distribution networks of existing providers supported by
44
government subsidized provision of equipment and subsidized pricing (Cross-Chapter Box 9).
45
46
Shifts in one country may spill over to other countries. Collective action by individuals as part of formal
47
social movements or informal lifestyle changes underpins system change (5.2.3, 5.4.1, 5.4.5.3, 13.5).
