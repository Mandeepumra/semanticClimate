4.3.2.5
New tools are needed to pave and assess development pathways
9
Relative to the literature on mitigation pathways described in 4.2.5 and in 4.3.3, the literature on
10
development pathways is limited. The climate research community has developed the Shared Socio-
11
economic pathways (SSPs) that link several socio-economic drivers including equity in relation to
12
welfare, resources, institutions, governance and climate mitigation policies in order to reflect many of
13
the key development directions (O’Neill et al. 2014). In most modelling exercises however,
14
development remains treated as an exogenous input. In addition, models may capture only some
15
dimensions of development that are relevant for mitigation options, thereby not capturing distributional
16
aspects and not allowing consistency checks with broader developmental goals (Valadkhani et al. 2016).
17
Quantitative tools for assessing mitigation pathways could be more helpful if they could provide
18
information on a broader range of development indicators, and could model substantively different
19
alternative development paths, thereby providing information on which levers might shift development
20
in a more sustainable direction.
21
Doing so requires new ways of thinking with interdisciplinary research and use of alternative
22
frameworks and methods suited to deeper understanding of change agents, determinants of change and
23
adaptive management among other issues (Winkler 2018). This includes, inter alia, being able to
24
examine enabling conditions for shifting development pathways (see 4.4.1); re-evaluating the neo-
25
classical assumptions within most models, both on the functioning of markets and on the behaviour of
26
agents, to better address obstacles on the demand side, obstacles on the supply side and market
27
distortions (Ekholm et al. 2013; Staub-Kaminski et al. 2014; Grubb et al. 2015) improving
28
representation of issues related with uncertainty, innovation, inertia and irreversibility within the larger
29
development contexts, including energy access and security ; improving the representation of social and
30
human capital, and of social, technological and governance innovations (Pedde et al. 2019).
31
Tools have been developed in that direction, for example in the Mitigation Action Plans and Scenarios
32
(MAPS) community (La Rovere et al. 2014b), but need to be further mainstreamed in the analysis.
33
Back-casting is often a preferred modelling approach for assessment aiming to align national
34
development goals with global climate goals like CO
2
stabilisation. Back-casting is a normative
35
approach where modellers construct desirable futures and specify upfront targets and then find out
36
possible pathways to attain these targets (IPCC et al. 2001). Use of approaches like back-casting are
37
useful not only in incorporating the long term national development objectives in the models, but also
38
evaluating conflicts and synergies more effectively (van der Voorn et al. 2020). In back casting, the
39
long-term national development objectives remain the key benchmarks guiding the model dynamics
40
and the global climate goal is interfaced to realise the co-benefits. The models then delineate the
41
roadmap of national actions such that the national goals are achieved with a comprehensive
42
understanding of the full costs and benefits of low carbon development (often including the costs of
43
adaptation and impacts from residual climate change). Back-casting modelling exercises show that
44
aligning development and climate actions could result in much lower ‘social cost of carbon’ (Shukla et
45
al. 2008). Back-casting does not aim to produce blueprints. Rather, it indicates the relative feasibility
46
and the social, environmental, and political implications of different development and climate futures
