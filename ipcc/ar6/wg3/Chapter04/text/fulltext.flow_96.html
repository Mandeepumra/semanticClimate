7
with bounded global mitigation benefits (Gattuso et al. 2018; Bindoff et al. 2019). Such
8

9

10

11

12

15

Coastal ecosystem-based mitigation can be cost-effective, but interventions should be designed with
17
care. One concern is to assure that actions remain effective at higher levels of climate change (Alongi
18
2015; Bindoff et al. 2019). Also, methane emissions from ecosystems may partially reduce the benefit
19

20

21
al. 2017), there may be entrenched interests opposing restoration and protection actions.

Restoration and protection of terrestrial ecosystems
23
Restoration of terrestrial landscapes can be a direct outcome of development pathways, and can be
24
critical to achieving a variety of SDGs (especially 1, 2, 6, 8, 13, 15) (Lapola et al. 2018; Vergara et al.
25
2016) although it also presents risks and can have trade-offs with other SDGs (Cao et al. 2010; Dooley
26
and Kartha 2018). Landscape restoration is nearly always a mitigation action, and can also provide
27
adaptive capacity. While policy in Brazil has tended to focus on the Amazon as a carbon sink, the
28
mitigation co-benefits of ecosystem-based adaptation actions have been highlighted in the literature (Di
29
Gregorio et al. 2016; Locatelli et al. 2011). A study of potential restoration of degraded lands in Latin
30
America (Vergara et al. 2016) indicates that substantial benefits for mitigation, adaptation, and
31
economic development accrue after several years, underscoring a reliance on deliberate development
32
choices. In agricultural contexts, restoration is a development choice that can enhance adaptive and
33
mitigative capacity via impact on farmer livelihoods.
34
Preventing degradation of landscapes can support both mitigation and adaptation (IPCC 2019).
35
Restoration of ecosystems is associated with improved water filtration, ground water recharge and flood
36
control and multiple other ecosystem services (Ouyang et al. 2016).
37
Restoration projects must be designed with care. There can be trade-offs in addition to the synergies
38
noted above (7.6.4.3). Restorations may be unsuccessful if not considered in their socio-economic
39
context (Lengefeld et al. 2020; Iftekhar et al. 2017; Jellinek et al. 2019). Restoration projects for
40
mitigation purposes can be more effective if done with adaptation in mind (Gray et al. 2011) as a
41
changing climate may render some mitigation actions biophysically infeasible (Arneth et al. 2021).
42
Landscape restoration projects intended for CDR may underperform due to future release of stored
43
carbon, or deferral of storage until after irreversible climate change effects (e.g. extinctions) (Dooley
44
and Kartha 2018).
