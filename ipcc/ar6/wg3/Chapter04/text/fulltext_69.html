Degree of inequality: Chapter 2 notes that while eradicating extreme poverty and providing universal
6
access to modern energy services to poor populations across the globe has negligible implications for
7
emissions growth, existing studies on the role of poverty and inequality as drivers of GHG emissions
8
provide limited evidence that under certain contexts greater inequality can lead to a deterioration in
9
environmental quality and may be associated with higher GHG emissions (2.4). In fact, factors affecting
10
household consumption based emissions include household size, age, education attainment,
11
employment status, urban vs rural location and housing stock (Druckman and Jackson 2015). There is
12
evidence to indicate that at the household level, the increase in emissions from additional consumption
13
of the lower income households could be larger than the reduction in emissions from the drop in
14
consumption from the high income households (Sager 2019). Accordingly, as countries seek to fulfil
15
the objective of reducing inequality, there are possibilities of higher increase in emissions (Sager 2019).
16
Since reducing inequality, as noted above, is globally one of the main development priorities, a large
17
body of literature focuses on the compatibility of climate change mitigation and reduction in economic
18
inequality (Berthe and Elie 2015; Grunewald et al. 2017; Hao et al. 2016; Wiedenhofer et al. 2017;
19
Auffhammer and Wolfram 2014; Baek and Gweisah 2013). However, the use of narrow approaches or
20
simple methods of studying the relationships of income inequality and emissions by looking at
21
correlations, may miss important linkages. For example, the influence of inequality on social values
22
such as status and civic mindedness and non-political interests that shape environmental policy can
23
influence overall consumption and its environmental impacts (Berthe and Elie 2015). Moreover,
24
inequalities may also be reflected in gender, education, racial and ethnic profiles and could accordingly
25
be associated with the level of emissions and mitigation prospects (Andrijevic et al. 2020).

The Illustrative Mitigation Pathways (IMP) developed for this Report (Box 3.1 and section 3.2.5)
27
provide another example of how development pathways influence mitigative capacity. Precisely,
28
IMP1.5-SP (Shifting Pathways) and 1.5-Ren (Renewables) lead to the same long-term temperature, but
29
differ in underlying socio-economic conditions. The former is based on Shared Socio-economic
30
Pathway (SSP) 1 (sustainable development), whereas the latter is based on SSP 2 (middle of the road).
31
Comparing 1.5-Ren to 1.5-SP can thus be interpreted as a numerical translation of trying the reach the
32
same long-term temperature goal without and with shifting development pathways towards
33
sustainability. Data shows that the global price of carbon necessary to remain on target is 40%-50%
34
lower in the latter relative to the former, thus indicating that mitigation is cheaper with a shift in
35
development pathway towards sustainability. Other cost indicators (e.g. consumption loss or GDP loss)
36
tell the same story. Since both IMPs were computed using the same underlying model, the comparison
37
is even more robust.

In sum, development pathways can lead to different emission levels and different capacities and
39
opportunities to mitigate (medium evidence, high agreement). Thus, focusing on shifting development
40
pathways can lead to larger systemic sustainability benefits.
