Mitigation efforts in subnational and non-state action plans and policies 11
The decision adopting the Paris Agreement stresses the importance of “stronger and more ambitious
12
climate action” by non-government and subnational stakeholders, “including civil society, the private
13
sector, financial institutions, cities and other subnational authorities, local communities and indigenous
14
peoples” (UNFCCC 2015e). The Marrakech Partnership for Global Action, launched in the 2016
15
UNFCCC Conference of Parties by two “high-level champions,” further formalized the contributions
16
of non-government and subnational actors taking action through seven thematic areas (e.g., energy,
17
human settlements, industry, land-use, etc.) and one cross-cutting area (resilience). Since then, non-
18
state actors, e.g., companies and civil society, and subnational actors, e.g. cities and regions, have
19
emerged to undertake a range of largely voluntary carbon mitigation actions (Hsu et al. 2019, 2018)
20
both as individual non-state actors (NSA in the following) and through national and international
21
cooperative initiatives (ICIs) (Hsu et al. 2018). ICIs take a variety of forms, ranging from those that
22
focus solely on non-state actors to those that engage national and even local governments. They can
23
also range in commitment level, from primarily membership-based initiatives that do not require
24
specific actions to those that require members to tackle emissions reductions in specific sectors or aim
25
for transformational change.
26
Quantification of the (potential) impact of these actions is still limited. Almost all studies estimate the
27
potential impact of the implementation of actions by NSA and ICI, but do not factor in that they may
28
not reach their targets. The main reason for this is that there is very limited data currently available from
29
individual actors (e.g., annual GHG inventory reports) and initiatives to assess their progress towards
30
their targets. A few studies have attempted to assess progress of initiatives by looking into the
31
initiatives’ production of relevant outputs (Chan et al. 2018). Quantification does not yet cover all
32
commitments and only a selected number of ICIs are analysed in the existing literature. Most of these
33
studies exclude commitments that are not (self-)identified as related to climate change mitigation, those
34
that are not connected to international networks, or those that are communicating in languages other
35
than English.
36
Non state action could make significant contributions to achieving the Paris climate goals (limited
37
evidence, high agreement). However, efforts to measure the extent to which non-state and subnational
38
actors go beyond national policy are still nascent (Kuramochi et al. 2020; Hsu et al. 2019) and we do
39
not fully understand the extent to which ambitious action by non-state actors is additional to what
40
national governments intend to do. Subnational and non-state climate action may also have benefits in
41
reinforcing, implementing, or piloting national policy, in place of or in addition to achieving additional
42
emissions reductions (Broekhoff et al. 2015; Heidrich et al. 2016; Hsu et al. 2017).
