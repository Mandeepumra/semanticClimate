Transformations run into oppositions
1
As noted above, shifting development pathways and accelerating mitigation involve a broad range of
2
stakeholders and decision-makers, at multiple geographical and temporal scales. They require a credible
3
and trusted process for reconciling perspectives and balancing potential side-effects, managing winners
4
and losers and implementing compensatory measures to ensure an inclusive just transition (Newell and
5
Mulvaney 2013; Miller and Richter 2014; Gambhir et al. 2018; Diffenbaugh and Burke 2019). Such
6
processes are designed to manage the risk of inequitable or non-representative power dynamics
7
(Helsinki Design Lab 2014; Kahane 2019; Boulle et al. 2015). More generally, stakeholder processes
8
can be subject to regulatory capture by special interests, or outright opposition from a variety of
9
stakeholders. Information asymmetry between government and business may shape the results of
10
consultative processes. Long experience of political management of change demonstrates that managing
11
such risks is not easy, and requires sufficiently strong and competent institutions (Stiglitz 1998). The
12
next section on Just Transition (4.5) addresses this issue.

4.5
Equity, including just transitions
16
Equity is an ethical and at times economic imperative, but it is also instrumentally an enabler of deeper
17
ambition for accelerated mitigation (Hoegh-Guldberg et al. 2019). The literature supports a range of
18
estimates of the net benefits—globally or nationally—of low-carbon transformation, and it identifies a
19
number of difficulties in drawing definitive quantitative conclusions (e.g., comparisons of costs &
20
benefits among different actors, the existence of non-economic impacts, comparison across time,
21
uncertainty in magnitude, 3.6). One of the most important of these dimensions is the distributional
22
consequences of mitigation, as well as a range of equity considerations arising from the uncertainty in
23
net benefits, as well as from the distribution of costs and benefits among winners and losers (Rendall
24
2019; Caney 2016; Lahn and Bradley 2016; Lenferna 2018a; Kartha et al. 2018b; Robiou Du Pont et
25
al. 2017). Some equity approaches are even just seeking corrective justice including for historical
26
emissions (Adler 2007). For an assessment of literature on fairness in NDCs, see 4.2.2.7.
27
Equity issues are often discussed in the literature via frameworks that are well-founded in the ethical
28
literature and that have a strong bearing on effort-sharing, but have not yet been quantitatively modelled
29
and expressed in the form of an emissions allocation quantified framework. These include, for example,
30
ethical perspectives based in human rights (Johl and Duyck 2012), human capabilities (Klinsky et al.
31
2017b), environmental justice (Mohai et al. 2009; Schlosberg 2009), ecological debt (Srinivasana et al.
32
2008; Warlenius et al. 2015), transitional justice (Klinsky 2017; Klinsky and Brankovic 2018), and
33
planetary boundaries (Häyhä et al. 2016).
34
While there is extensive literature on equity frameworks for national emissions allocations (CSO Equity
35
Review 2018, 2015, 2017; Kemp-Benedict et al. 2018; Pye et al. 2020; Robiou du Pont and
36
Meinshausen 2018; Fyson et al. 2020; Holz et al. 2018; Pozo et al. 2020), such studies have tended to
37
focus on allocation of a global carbon budget among countries based on quantified equity frameworks.
38
The implicit normative choices made in these analysis have limitations (Kartha et al. 2018a). Moreover,
39
there are many ethical parameters that could be introduced to enrich the existing quantitative
40
frameworks, such as progressivity (Holz et al. 2018), consumption-based accounting (Afionis et al.
41
2017), prioritarianism (Adler and Treich 2015), and a right to development (Moellendorf 2020).
42
