4.2.6.3
Mitigation and employment in the short- and medium-term
13
Numerous studies have analysed the potential impact of carbon pricing on labour markets. Chateau et
14
al. (2018) and OECD (2017a) find that the implementation of green policies globally (defined broadly
15
as policies that internalise environmental externalities through taxes and other tools, shifting
16
profitability from polluting to green sectors) need not harm total employment, and that the broad skill
17
composition (low-, high- and medium-skilled jobs) of emerging and contracting sectors is very similar,
18
with the largest shares of job creation and destruction at the lowest skill level. To smoothen the labour
19
market transition, they conclude that it may be important to reduce labour taxes, to compensate
20
vulnerable households, and to provide education and training programs, the latter making it easier for
21
labour to move to new jobs. Consistent with this, other studies that simulate the impact of scenarios
22
with more or less ambitious mitigation policies (including 100% reliance on renewable energy by 2050)
23
find relatively small (positive or negative) impacts on aggregate global employment that are more
24
positive if labour taxes are reduced but encompass substantial losses for sectors and regions that today
25
are heavily dependent on fossil fuels (Arndt et al. 2013; Huang et al. 2019; Vandyck et al. 2016;
26
Jacobson et al. 2019). Among worker categories, low-skilled workers tend to suffer wage losses as they
27
are more likely to have to reallocate, something that can come at a cost in the form of a wage cut
28
(assuming that workers who relocate are initially less productive than those who already work in the
29
sector). The results for alternative carbon revenue recycling schemes point to trade-offs: a reduction in
30
labour taxes often leads to the most positive employment outcomes while lump-sum (uniform per-
31
capita) transfers to households irrespective of income yield a more egalitarian outcome.
32
The results from country-level studies using CGE models tend be similar to those at global level.
33
Aggregate employment impacts are small and may be positive especially if labour taxes are cut, see
34
e.g., (Telaye et al. 2019) for Ethiopia, (Kolsuz and Yeldan 2017) for Turkey, (Fragkos et al. 2017) for
35
the EU, (Mu et al. 2018b) for China. On the other hand, sectoral reallocations away from fossil-
36
dependent sectors may be substantial, see e.g., (Alton et al. 2014) for South Africa or (Huang et al.
37
2019) for China. Targeting of investment to labour-intensive green sectors may generate the strongest
38
employment gains, see, e.g., (Perrier and Quirion 2018) for France, (van Meijl et al. 2018) for the
39
Netherlands, (Patrizio et al. 2018) for the USA. Changes in skill requirements between emerging and
40
declining sectors appear to be quite similar, involving smaller transitions than during the IT revolution
41
(Bowen et al. 2018).
42
In sum, the literature suggests that the employment impact of mitigation policies tends to be limited on
43
aggregate, but can be significant at the sectoral level (medium evidence, medium agreement) and that
44
cutting labour taxes may limit adverse effects on employment (limited evidence, medium agreement).
