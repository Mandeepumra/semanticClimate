4.4.2.1
Development pathways can build greater capacity for both adaptation and mitigation
10
Previous IPCC assessments have reflected on making development more sustainable (Fleurbaey et al.
11
2014; Sathaye et al. 2007; IPCC et al. 2001). Other assessments have highlighted how ecosystem
12
functions can support sustainable development and are critical to meeting the goals of the Paris
13
Agreement (IPBES 2019b). IPCC SR15 found that sustainable development pathways to 1.5 °C broadly
14
support and often enable transformations and that “sustainable development has the potential to
15
significantly reduce systemic vulnerability, enhance adaptive capacity, and promote livelihood security
16
for poor and disadvantaged populations (high confidence)” (IPCC 2018b: 5.3.1). With careful
17
management, shifting development pathways can build greater adaptive and mitigative capacity, as
18
further confirmed in recent literature (Schramski et al. 2018; Harvey et al. 2014; Ebi et al. 2014;
19
Rosenbloom et al. 2018; Antwi-Agyei et al. 2015; Singh 2018; IPBES 2019b). The literature points to
20
the challenge of design of specific policies and shifts in development pathways to achieve both
21
mitigation and adaptation goals.
22
Governance and Institutional capacity
23
Governance and institutional capacity necessary for mitigation actions also enables effective adaptation
24
actions. Implementation of mitigation and adaptation actions can, however, encounter different sets of
25
challenges. Mitigation actions requiring a shift away from established sectors and resources (e.g., fossil
26
fuels) entail governance challenges to overcome vested interests (SEI et al. 2020; Piggot et al. 2020).
27
Mitigation-focused initiatives from non-state actors tend to attain greater completion than adaptation-
28
focused initiatives (NewClimate Institute et al. 2019).
29
Behaviour and lifestyles
30
On the level of individual entities, adaptation is reactive to current or anticipated environmental changes
31
but mitigation is undertaken deliberately. Chapter 5 considers behavioural change, including the
32
reconsideration of values and what is meant by well-being, and reflecting on a range of actors addressing
33
both adaptation and mitigation. Shifting development pathways may be disruptive (Cross Chapter Box
34
5), and there may be limits to propensity to change. Some studies report that climate change deniers
35
and sceptics can be induced to undertake pro-environmental action if those actions are framed in terms
36
of societal welfare, not climate change (Bain et al. 2012; Hornsey et al. 2016). Concrete initiatives to
37
change behaviour and lifestyles include the Transition Town movement, which seeks to implement a
38
just transition—both in relation to adaptation and mitigation—in specific localities (Roy et al. 2018).
