4.3.1.2
Shifting development pathways
3
Development pathways evolve as the result of the countless decisions and actions at all levels of societal
4
structure, as well due to the emergent dynamics within and between institutions, cultural norms, socio-
5
technological systems, and the biogeophysical environment. Society can choose to make decisions and
6
take actions with the shared intention of influencing the future development pathway toward specific
7
agreed objectives.
8
The SDGs provide a lens on diverse national and local development objectives. Humankind currently
9
faces multiple sustainability challenges that together present global society with the challenge of
10
assessing, deliberating, and attempting to bring about a viable, positive future development pathway.
11
Ecological sustainability challenges include reducing GHG emissions, protecting the ozone, controlling
12
pollutants such as aerosols and persistent organics, managing nitrogen and phosphorous cycles, etc.
13
(Steffen et al. 2015), which are necessary to address the rising risks to biodiversity and ecosystem
14
services on which humanity depends (IPBES 2019a). Socioeconomic sustainability challenges include
15
conflict, persistent poverty and deprivation, various forms of pervasive and systemic discrimination and
16
deprivation, and socially corrosive inequality. The global adoption of the SDGs and their underlying
17
indicators (United Nations 2018) reflect a negotiated prioritisation of these common challenges.
18
Figure 4.7 illustrates the process of shifting development pathways. The lines illustrate different
19
possible development pathways through time, some of which (shown here toward the top of the figure)
20
remove obstacles to the adoption and effective implementation of sustainable development policies,
21
and thus give access to a rich policy toolbox for accelerating mitigation and achieving SDGs. Other
22
development pathways (shown here toward the bottom of the figure) do not overcome, or even reinforce
23
the obstacles to adopting and effectively implementing sustainable development policies, and thus leave
24
decision-makers with more limited policy toolbox (4.2.7; Figure 4.6). A richer tool box enables faster,
25
deeper and broader mitigation.
26
The development pathways branch and branch again, signifying how a diversity of decision-makers
27
(policy makers, organizations, investors, voters, consumers, etc.) are continuously making choices that
28
influence which of many potential development pathways society follows. Some of these choices fall
29
clearly within the domain of mitigation policy. For example, what level carbon price, if any, should be
30
imposed? Should fossil fuel subsidies be removed? Most decisions, of course, fall outside the direct
31
domain of mitigation policy. Shifting development pathways toward sustainability involves this broader
32
realm of choices beyond mitigation policy per se, and requires identifying those choices that are
33
important determinants of the existing obstacles to accelerating mitigation and meeting other SDGs.
34
Addressing these choices coherently shifts the development pathway away from a continuation of
35
existing trends,
