4.4.3
Risks and uncertainties 5
Shifting development pathways and accelerating mitigation are complex endeavours that carry risks.
6
Some of these risks can be easily captured by quantitative models. Others are better understood via
7
qualitative approaches, such as qualitative narrative storylines (told in words) and methods mixing
8
qualitative and quantitative models (Kemp-Benedict 2012; Hanger-Kopp et al. 2019). The following
9
outline key risks and relevant hedging strategies identified in the literature.
10
4.4.3.1
Actions by others not consistent with domestic efforts
11
The international context is a major source of uncertainty for national-level planning, especially for
12
small- or medium-sized open economies, because the outcome of domestic choices may significantly
13
depend on decisions made by other countries and actor, over which national governments have limited
14
or no control (Lachapelle and Paterson 2013). Availability of foreign financial resources in countries
15
with limited domestic savings (Baum et al. 2017) and availability of technology transfers (Glachant and
16
Dechezleprêtre 2017) are some examples. Other external decisions with significant bearing on domestic
17
action include mitigation policies in other countries (Dai et al. 2017), and especially in major trading
18
partners, the lack of which can result in competitive disadvantage for sectors exposed to international
19
competition (Alton et al. 2014). The international prices of the key commodities (notably energy), goods
20
and services are important, notably when shifting development pathway is based on structural change
21
(e.g., Willenbockel et al. (2017) for Ghana and Kenya).
22
Remedies include first devising policy packages that are, to the extent possible, robust to uncertainty
23
regarding external decisions. For example, mitigation in the building sector is considered less
24
problematic for competitiveness since the construction sector is less exposed to international
25
competition. Remedies also include securing international cooperation to reduce the uncertainty that
26
domestic decision-makers face about the international context. Shifting investments towards low-GHG
27
solutions requires a combination of conducive public policies, attractive investment opportunities and
28
financing of transitions (15.6), which can enable shifting development pathways. Cooperation can
29
generate positive spill overs through technology diffusion (13.6.6). Third, cooperation is not limited to
30
governments. As discussed in section 4.2.3, international cooperative initiatives among non-State actors
31
(cities, economic branches, etc.) can also provide know-how, resources and stable cooperative
32
frameworks that reduce uncertainty for individual actors (14.5.5).

4.4.3.2
Parts of complex policy packages fail
34
As outlined in the examples in section 4.4.1 above, shifting development pathways and accelerating
35
mitigation are complex endeavours, on which there is limited experience and know-how from the past.
36
An uncertainty is that parts of these policy packages may fail, i.e., under-deliver relative to the amount
37
of mitigation and of transformations initially expected. For example, France has failed to meet its 2015-
38
2018 carbon budget as housing retrofitting programs, in particular, have failed to deliver the expected
39
amount of emission reductions (Haut Conseil pour le Climat 2019). There are two main options to tackle
40
this risk. The first is to build in redundancy. The second is to anticipate that some parts of the policies
41
will inevitably fail, and build-in monitoring and corrective mechanisms in a sequential decision-making
42
process. To this regard, building institutions that can properly monitor, learn from and improve over
43
time is critical (Nair and Howlett 2017).
