Innovation and technologies
5
Systems transitions that address both adaptation and mitigation include the widespread adoption of new
6
and possibly disruptive technologies and practices and enhanced climate-driven innovation (IPCC
7
2018a). See Chapter 16 for an in-depth discussion of innovation and technology transfer. The literature
8
points to trade-offs that developing countries face in investing limited resources in research and
9
development, though finding synergies in relation to agriculture (Adenle et al. 2015). Other studies
10
point to difference in technology transfers for adaptation and mitigation (Biagini et al. 2014).
11
Adaptation projects tend to use existing technologies whereas mitigation climate actions are more likely
12
to rely on novel technologies. Innovations for mitigation are typically technology transfers from
13
developed to less-developed countries (Biagini et al. 2014), however this so-called North-South
14
technology transfer pathway is not exclusive (Biagini et al. 2014), and is increasingly challenged by
15
China’s global role in implementing mitigation actions (Chen 2018; Urban 2018). Indigenous
16
knowledge can be a unique source for techniques for adaptation (Nyong et al. 2007) and may be
17
favoured over externally generated knowledge (Tume et al. 2019).

Policy
19
Adaptation-focused pathways might reduce inequality, if adequate support is available and well-
20
distributed (Pelling and Garschagen 2019). Some studies suggest that cities might plan for possible
21
synergies in adaptation and mitigation strategies, currently done independently (Grafakos et al. 2019).
22
The literature suggests that cities might identify both mitigation and adaptation as co-benefits of
23
interventions targeted at developmental goals (Dulal 2017).

4.4.2.2
Specific links between mitigation and adaptation
25
Mitigation actions can be adaptive and vice-versa. In particular, many nature-based solutions (NBS) for
26
climate mitigation are adaptive (medium evidence, medium agreement). Multiple NBS are being
27
pursued under current development pathways (see Chapter 7), but shifting to sustainable development
28
pathways may enable a wider set of nature-based mitigation solutions with adaptation benefits. An
29
example of this would be a shift to more sustainable diets through guidelines, carbon taxes, or
30
investment in R&D of animal product substitutes (Figure 13.2) which could reduce pressure on land
31
and allow for implementation of multiple NBS. Many of these solutions are consistent with meeting
32
other societal goals, including biodiversity conservation and other sustainable development goals
33
(Griscom et al. 2017; Tallis et al. 2018; Fargione et al. 2018). However, there can be synergies and
34
trade-offs in meeting a complex set of sustainability goals (e.g., biodiversity, see 7.6.5 and 3.1.5).
35
Development is a key factor leading to land degradation in many parts of the world (IPBES 2019b).
36
Shifting development pathways to sustainability can include restoration and protection of ecosystems,
37
which can enhance capacity for both mitigation and adaptation actions (IPBES 2019b).
38
In this section, we explore mitigation actions related to sustainable agriculture, coastal ecosystems
39
(“blue carbon”), and restoration and protection of some terrestrial ecosystems. These mitigation actions
40
are exemplary of trade-offs and synergies with adaptation, sensitivity to biophysical coupling, and
41
linkages to development pathways. Other specific examples can be found in Chapters 6 to 11.
