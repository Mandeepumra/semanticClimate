Development pathways shape emissions and capacities to mitigate
2
Analysis in the mitigation literature often frames mitigation policy as having development co-benefits,
3
the main objective being climate stabilisation. This misses the point that development drives emissions,
4
and not vice versa, and it is the overall development approach and policies that determine mitigation
5
pathways (Munasinghe 2007). A large body of literature supports the fact that development pathways
6
have direct and, just as importantly, indirect implications for GHG emissions (Nakicenovic et al. 2000;
7
Winkler 2017b), through multiple channels, such as the nature of economic activity, spatial patterns of
8
development, degree of inequality, and population growth.
9
Economic structure: Chapter 2 notes that overall, affluence (GDP per capita), economic growth and
10
population growth have remained the main upward drivers of CO
2
emissions from fossil-fuel
11
combustion in the past decade, with energy efficiency the main countervailing force (2.4) (Wang and
12
Feng 2017; Lin and Liu 2015). A major component of the development pathway of a country is precisely
13
the nature of the economic activities on which the country relies (e.g., agriculture and mining, heavy
14
industry, services, high-tech products, etc.) as well as the way it articulates its economy with the rest of
15
the World (e.g., export-led growth vs. import substitution strategies). Hence, the development pathway
16
ultimately drives the underlying structure of the economy, and to a large degree the relationship between
17
activity and GHG emissions.
18
At country level, however, the picture is more nuanced. Both India and China show signs of relative
19
decoupling between GDP and emissions because of structural change (Chen et al. 2018a). Sumabat et
20
al. (2016) indicate that economic growth had a negative impact on CO
2
emissions in Philippines. Baek
21
and Gweisah (2013) find that CO
2
emissions tend to drop monotonously as incomes increased. Lantz
22
and Feng (2006) also indicate that per capita GDP is not related to CO
2
emissions in Canada. Other
23
studies point to an emerging consensus that the relationship between CO
2
emissions and economic
24
indicators depends on the level of development of countries (Nguyen and Kakinaka 2019; Sharma
25
2011). While some literature indicates that absolute decoupling of economic growth and GHG
26
emissions has occurred in some countries (Le Quéré et al. 2019), a larger systematic review found
27
limited evidence of this (Haberl et al. 2020).
28
Looking ahead, choices about the nature of economic activities are expected to have significant
29
implications for emissions. For example, a development pathway that focuses on enhancing economic
30
growth based on manufacturing is likely to lead to very different challenges for mitigation compared to
31
one that focuses on services-led growth. (Quéré et al. 2018) find that choices about whether or not to
32
export offshore oil in Brazil will have significant implications for the country’s GHG emissions.
33
Similarly, in China, transforming industrial structure towards tertiary sectors (Kwok et al. 2018) and
34
restructuring exports towards higher value-added products (Wu et al. 2019) are expected to have
35
significant implications for GHG emissions.
36

37
countries leads to increased CO
2
emissions, the substantial migration of rural populations to urban areas
38
in these countries being the main factor leading to increased levels of income and expenditure of new
39
urban dwellers which in turn leads to increased personal carbon footprints and overall emissions (2.4).
40

41

43
