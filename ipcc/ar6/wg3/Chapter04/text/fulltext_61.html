4.3.1.1
What are development pathways?
3
The term development pathway is defined in various ways in the literature, and these definitions
4
invariably refer to the evolution over time of a society’s defining features. A society’s development
5
pathway can be described, analysed, and explained from a variety of perspectives, capturing a range of
6
possible features, trends, processes, and mechanisms. It can be examined in terms of specific
7
quantitative indicators, such as population, urbanisation level, life expectancy, literacy rate, GDP,
8
carbon dioxide emission rate, average surface temperature, etc. Alternately, it can be described with
9
reference to trends and shifts in broad socio-political or cultural features, such as democratisation,
10
liberalisation, colonisation, globalisation, consumerism, etc. Or, it can be described in a way that
11
highlights and details a particular domain of interest; for example, as an “economic pathway”,
12
“technological pathway”, “demographic pathway”, or others. Any such focused description of a
13
pathway is more limited, by definition, than the general and encompassing notion of a development
14
pathway.
15
Development pathways represent societal evolution over time, and can be assessed retrospectively and
16
interpreted in a historical light, or explored prospectively by anticipating and assessing alternative future
17
pathways. Development pathways, and prospective development pathways in particular, can reflect
18
societal objectives, as in “low-emission development pathways”, “climate-resilient development
19
pathways”, “sustainable development pathways”, “inclusive development pathway”, and as such can
20
embed normative assumptions or preferences, or can reflect potential dystopian futures to be avoided.
21
A national development plan (4.3.2) is a representation of a possible development pathway for a given
22
society reflecting its objectives, as refracted through its development planning process.
23
