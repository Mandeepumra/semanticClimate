4.2.6.4
Mitigation and equity in the near- and mid-term
5
Climate mitigation may exacerbate socio-economic pressures on poorer households (Jakob et al. 2014).
6
First, the price increase in energy-intensive goods and services—including food (Hasegawa et al.
7
2018)—associated with mitigation may affect poorer households disproportionally (Bento 2013), and
8
increase the number of energy-poor (Berry 2019). Second, the mitigation may disproportionally affect
9
low-skilled workers (see previous section). Distributional issues have been identified not only with
10
explicit price measures (carbon tax, emission permits system, subsidy removal), but also with subsidies
11
for renewables (Borenstein and Davis 2016), and efficiency and emissions standards (Davis and Knittel
12
2019; Bruegge et al. 2019; Levinson 2019; Fullerton and Muehlegger 2019).
13
Distributional implications, however, are context specific, depending on consumption patterns (initially
14
and ease of adjusting them in response to price changes) and asset ownership (see for example analysis
15
of energy prices in Indonesia by Renner et al. (2019)). In an analysis of the distributional impact of
16
carbon pricing based on household expenditure data for 87 low- and middle-income countries, Dorband
17
et al. (2019) find that, in countries with a per-capita income of up to USD15,000 per capita (PPP
18
adjusted), carbon pricing has a progressive impact on income distribution and that there may be an
19
inversely U-shaped relationship between energy expenditure shares and per-capita income, rendering
20
carbon pricing regressive in high-income countries, i.e., in countries where the capacity to pursue
21
compensatory policies tends to be relatively strong.
22
The literature finds that the detailed design of mitigation policies is critical for their distributional
23
impacts (robust evidence, high agreement). For example, Vogt-Schilb et al. (2019) suggest to turn to
24
cash transfer programs, established as some of the most efficient tools for poverty reduction in
25
developing countries. In an analysis of Latin America and the Caribbean, they find that allocation of 30
26
percent of carbon revenues would suffice to compensate poor and vulnerable households on average,
27
leaving the rest for other uses. This policy tool is not only available in countries with relatively high
28
per-capita incomes: in Sub-Saharan Africa, where per-capita incomes are relatively low, cash transfer
29
programs have been implemented in almost all countries ((Beegle et al. 2018), p. 57), and are found
30
central to the success of energy subsidy reforms (Rentschler and Bazilian 2017). In the same vein,
31
Böhringer et al. (2021) finds that recycling of revenues from emissions pricing in equal amounts to
32
every household appeals as an attractive strategy to mitigate regressive effects and thereby make
33
stringent climate policy more acceptable on societal fairness grounds. However, distributional gains
34
from such recycling may come at the opportunity cost of not reaping efficiency gains from reductions
35
in the taxes that are most distortionary (Goulder et al. 2019).
36
Distributional concerns related to climate mitigation are also prevalent in developed countries, as
37
demonstrated, for instance, by France’s recent yellow-vest movement, which was ignited by an increase
38
in carbon taxes. It exemplifies the fact that, when analysing the distributional effects of carbon pricing,
39
it is not sufficient to consider vertical redistribution (i.e., redistribution between households at different
40
incomes levels but also horizontal redistribution (i.e., redistribution between households at similar
41
incomes which is due to differences in terms of spending shares and elasticities for fuel consumption).
42
Compared to vertical redistribution, it is more difficult to devise policies that effectively address
43
horizontal redistribution (Douenne 2020; Cronin et al. 2019; Pizer and Sexton 2019). However, it has
44
been shown ex post that transfer schemes considering income levels and location could have protected
45
or even improved the purchasing power of the bottom half of the population (Bureau et al. 2019).
