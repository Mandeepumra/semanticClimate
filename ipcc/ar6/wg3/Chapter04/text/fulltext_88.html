Example: Embedding carbon finance in broader fiscal reforms offers a way to mitigate and
3
rethink the social contract
4
In many countries, fiscal systems are currently under stress to provide resources for the implementation
5
of development priorities, such as, for example, providing universal health coverage and other social
6
services (Meheus and McIntyre 2017) or sustainably funding pension systems in the context of aging
7
populations (Asher and Bali 2017; Cruz-Martinez 2018). Overall, Baum et al. (2017) argue that low-
8
income countries are likely not to have the fiscal space to undertake the investment entailed in reaching
9
the SDGs. To create additional fiscal space, major options include improving tax recovery, reducing
10
subsidies and levying additional taxes.
11
Mitigation offers an opportunity to create additional fiscal space, and thus to serve the objectives
12
outlined above, by creating a new source of revenue for the government via carbon taxation or emissions
13
permit auctioning and by reducing existing expenditures via reduction in subsidies to fossil-fuel. The
14
1991 tax reform in Sweden is an early example in which environmental taxation (including, but not
15
limited to, fossil fuel taxation) was introduced as part of a package primarily aimed at lowering the
16
marginal tax rates (more than 80% at the time), at reducing other taxes, while keeping most of the
17
welfare state. To do so, the tax base was broadened, including through environmental and carbon
18
taxation (Sterner 2007). Once in place, the carbon tax rate was substantially ramped up over time, and
19
its base broadened (Criqui et al. 2019).
20
The future potential for using carbon taxation as a way to provide space for fiscal reform has been
21
highlighted in the so-called “green fiscal reform” literature (Vogt-Schilb et al. 2019). The potential is
22
large, since only 13 percent of global GHG emissions were covered by carbon pricing schemes in 2019
23
(Watts et al. 2019) and since many countries price carbon negatively by subsidising fossil fuel use, thus
24
generating effects that are the opposite of those that positive carbon prices hope to promote. In 2018,
25
the global subsidy value amounted to $427 billion, i.e., some 10 times the payment for carbon use
26
(Watts et al. 2019). However, the size of the potential for creating fiscal space varies strongly across
27
countries given differences in terms of current carbon prices and fuel subsidies.
28
The limited adoption of and political support for carbon pricing may be explained by the fact that most
29
of the gains occur in the future and depend on actions across the globe, making them seem abstract and
30
unpredictable, whereas the costs in the form of higher carbon prices are immediate (Karapin 2016).
31
Furthermore, the links between carbon pricing and emissions may not be clear to the public who, in
32
addition, may not trust that the government will use budgetary savings according to stated plans. The
33
latter may be due to various factors, including a history of limited government commitment and
34
corruption (Maestre-Andrés et al 2019 ; Withana & Sirini 2016; Chadwick 2017).
35
The literature reports limited systematic evidence based on ex post analysis of the performance of
36
carbon pricing–carbon taxes and greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions trading systems (ETSs) (Haites
37
2018). Performance assessment is complicated by the effect of other policies and exogenous factors.
38
(Haites 2018) suggests that since 2008, other policies have probably contributed more to emission
39
reductions than carbon taxes, and most tax rates are too low to achieve mitigation objectives. Emissions
40
under ETSs have declined, with the exception of four systems without emissions caps (ibid). Every
41
jurisdiction with an ETS and/or carbon tax also has other policies that affect its GHG emissions.
42
