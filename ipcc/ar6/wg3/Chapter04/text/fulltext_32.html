The literature frequently refers to national mitigation pathways up to 2030 or 2050 using long-term
19
temperature limits in the Paris Agreement (i.e., “2°C” or “1.5°C scenario”). Without additional
20
information, such denomination is incorrect. Working Group I reaffirmed “with high confidence the
21
AR5 finding that there is a near-linear relationship between cumulative anthropogenic CO
2
emissions
22
and the global warming they cause” (WGI SPM AR6). It is not the function of any single country’s
23
mitigation efforts, nor any individual actor’s. Emission pathways of individual countries or sectors in
24
the near- to mid-term can only be linked to a long-term temperature with additional assumptions
25
specifying (i) the GHG emissions and removals of other countries up the mid-term; and (ii) the GHG
26
emissions and removals of all countries beyond the near- and mid-term. For example, a national
27
mitigation pathway can be labelled “2°C compatible” if it derives from a global mitigation pathway
28
consistent with 2°C via an explicit effort sharing scheme across countries (see 4.2.2.6 and 4.5).

4.2.4.1
GHG Mitigation target under UNFCCC and Paris Agreement
34
The Paris Agreement requests that Parties should strive to formulate and communicate long-term low
35
GHG development strategies by 2020. (Note that by “long-term”, the UNFCCC means 2050, which is
36
the end point of the “mid-term” horizon range in the present report.) As of August 25, 2021, 31 countries
37
and the European Union had submitted low-emissions development strategies (LEDS) (Table 4.5).
38
By 2018, most long-term strategies targeted 80% emissions reduction in 2050 relative to a reference
39
(1990, 2000 or 2005). After IPCC SR1.5 was published, the number of the countries aiming at net zero
40
CO
2
or GHG emissions has been increasing.
