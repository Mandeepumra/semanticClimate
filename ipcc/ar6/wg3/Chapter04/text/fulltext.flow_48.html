4.2.5.8
Electrifying transport
1
Electrification of transport in tandem with power sector decarbonisation is expected to be a key strategy
2
for deep CO
2
mitigation in many countries. Passenger transport and light duty freight can already be
3
electrified, but electrifying heavy-duty road transport and fuel switching in aviation and shipping are
4
much more difficult and have not been addressed in most of the recent research.
5
In Germany, widespread electrification of private vehicles is expected by 2030 (Schmid and Knopf
6
2012) while for the EU-28, 50% overall transport electrification (excluding feedstock) and 75%
7
electrification of road transport is needed to reach net carbon neutrality according to (Duscha et al.
8
2019). In addition, novel fuels such as hydrogen, synthetic hydrocarbons and sustainable biogenic fuels
9
are needed to decarbonise aviation and water transport to achieve net carbon neutrality (Duscha et al.
10
2019).
11
In India, electrification, hydrogen, and biofuels are key to decarbonising the transport sector (Dhar et
12
al. 2018; Mittal et al. 2018; Vishwanathan et al. 2018b; Mathur and Shekhar 2020). Under a 1.5°C
13
scenario, nearly half of the light-duty passenger vehicle stock needs to be electrified according to
14
(Parikh et al. 2018). In China, a 1.5°C-compatible pathway would requires electrification of 2/5
th
of
15
transport (Jiang et al. 2018; China National Renewable Energy Centre 2019).
16
Similarly, in Canada, electrification of 59% of light-duty trucks and 23% of heavy-duty trucks are
17
needed as part of overall strategy to reduce CO
2
emissions by 80% by 2050. In addition, hydrogen is
18
expected to play a major role by accounting for nearly one-third of light-duty trucks, 68% of heavy-
19
duty trucks, and 33% of rail by 2050 according to Hammond et al. (2020).

4.2.5.9
Urban form meets information technology
21
Beyond technological measures, some densely populated countries including Germany, Japan, and
22
India are exploring using information technology/internet-of-things (IOT) to support mode-shifting and
23
reduce mobility demand through broader behaviour and lifestyle changes (Aggarwal 2017; Ashina et
24
al. 2012; Canzler and Wittowsky 2016; Dhar et al. 2018; Vishwanathan et al. 2018b). In Japan,
25
accelerated mitigation pathways consider the use of information technology and IOT to transform
26
human behaviour and transition to a sharing economy (Ashina et al. 2012; Oshiro et al. 2017a, 2018).
27
In Germany, one study points to including electromobility information and communication technologies
28
in the transport sector as key (Canzler and Wittowsky 2016) while another emphasise shifting from
29
road to rail transport, and reduced distances travelled as other possible transport strategies (Schmid and
30
Knopf 2012). India’s transport sector strategies also include use of information technology and the
31
internet, a transition to a sharing economy, and increasing infrastructure investment (Dhar et al. 2018;
32
Vishwanathan et al. 2018b). Behaviour and lifestyle change along with stakeholder integration in
33
decision-making are considered key to implementing new transport policies (Aggarwal 2017; Dhar et
34
al. 2018).
