4.1
Introduction
1
The recent IPCC Report on Global Warming of 1.5C (SR15) made clear that the next three decades
2
are critical if we are to achieve the long-term mitigation goal of the Paris Agreement (IPCC 2018a).
3
The present Chapter assesses the literature on mitigation and development pathways over that
4
timeframe, in the near- (up to 2030) and mid-term (up to 2050).
5
It considers three questions: (1) Where are we heading now? That is, what is the current state of affairs
6
with respect to climate mitigation and how did we get here? (2) Where do we want to go? I.e., what
7
state of affairs would meet the objectives of the Paris Agreement and achieving the Sustainable
8
Development Goals (SDGs)? and (3) How do we bring about this shift? I.e., what interventions are at
9
societies’ disposal to bring about the necessary change in an equitable manner?
10
Where are we heading now? Despite the drop in emissions due to the COVID-19 crisis, the gap
11
between projected emissions based on Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) in 2030 and
12
emissions pathways compatible with the long term temperature goal set in the Paris Agreement remains
13
large (4.2.2). In addition to this persistent emissions gap, we face an implementation gap, as current
14
policies are insufficient to achieve mitigation targets in NDCs, and sufficient international support is
15
not yet available to developing countries who have requested and quantified support needs. Continuing
16
along a development pathway characterized by the same underlying drivers, structural obstacles and
17
insufficient enabling conditions that led to high emissions will not address the problem (robust
18
evidence, high agreement).
19
The analysis of the gap is conducted together with Chapter 3 (see Cross-Chapter Box 4). Chapter 3 is
20
working backward, assessing mitigation in the long-term (beyond 2050 up to 2100) to draw the near-
21
and mid-term implications of long-term temperature and mitigations goals. Chapter 4, on the other hand,
22
works forward from current and planned mitigation (including NDCs) (4.2.1, 4.2.2) and from current
23
development paths to assess the implications for near- and mid-term Greenhouse Gases (GHG)
24
emissions and development goals. Some countries, regions, cities, communities and non-state actors are
25
taking leadership in implementing more ambitious action (4.2.3). This chapter also assesses national
26
low emission development strategies (4.2.4).
27
Where do we want to go? Technical alternatives and policy options exist to bridge the emissions and
28
implementation gaps, and the literature illustrates these with a wide range of accelerated techno-
29
economic pathways that deepen decarbonisation closer to the pace and scale required (4.2.5), and
30
examines their impacts on other development objectives (4.2.6). In practice, however, scaling up at the
31
broader, deeper, and faster level required to meet climate goals while advancing other development
32
objectives regularly faces prohibitive obstacles (4.2.7). Mitigation policies grafted on to existing
33
development pathways are unlikely to achieve rapid and deep emission reductions.
34
Secondly, even if carefully designed, climate policies to accelerate mitigation may have adverse
35
consequences for other development objectives. As a complement to mitigation action, taking action to
36
shift development pathways towards sustainability broadens the range of mitigation options, while
37
increasing the possibility to meet other development priorities at the same time (medium evidence, high
38
agreement).
39

40
constitute a thread throughout the report (see glossary entry on development pathways). The WGII
41
Report highlights the related concept of climate resilient development pathways (Chapter 18). Cross-
42
Chapter Box 5 on shifting sustainable pathway towards sustainability elaborates on the concept. The
43
influence of development pathways on emissions and mitigative capacity is discussed in Chapter 2.
