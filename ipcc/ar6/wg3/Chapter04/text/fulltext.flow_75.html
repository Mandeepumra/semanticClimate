Development pathways drive GHG emissions for a large part (2.4, 2.5 and 2.6). For example, different
4
social choices and policy packages with regard to land use and associated rents will result in human
5

6

7

There is compelling evidence to show that continuing along existing development pathways is unlikely
10
to achieve rapid and deep emission reductions (robust evidence, medium agreement). For example,
11
investments in long-lived infrastructure, including energy supply systems, could lock-in high emissions
12
pathways and risk making deep decarbonisation and sustainable policies more difficult and expensive.
13
Development pathways also determine the set of tools available to mitigate climate change (Figure 4.7).
14
For example, the capacity of households to move closer to their workplace, in response to, e.g., a price
15
signal on carbon and thus on gasoline, depends on rents, which themselves depend on the spatial
16
patterns of development of human settlements (8.3.1). Said differently, mitigation costs depend on past
17
development choices. Similarly, development pathways determine the enablers and levers available for
18
adaptation (WGII, Chapter 18) and for achieving other SDGs.
19
20
In the absence of shifts in development pathways, conventional mitigation policy instruments (e.g.,
21
carbon tax, emission quotas, technological norms, etc.) may not be able to limit emissions to a degree
22
sufficient for deep decarbonisation or only at very high economic and social costs.
23
24
Policies to shift development pathways, on the contrary, make mitigation policies more effective. For
25
example, policies that prioritise non-car transit, or limit rents close to work places would make it easier
26
for households to relocate in response to a price signal on transport, and thus makes the same degree of
27
mitigation achievable at lower economic and social cost.

30

31
Second, societies pursue a variety of development objectives, of which protecting the Earth’s climate
32
is part. The SDGs provide a global mapping of these goals. Absent climate mitigation, our collective
33
ability to achieve the SDGs in 2030 and to sustain them beyond 2030 is likely to be compromised, even
34
if adaptation measures are put in place (WGII).
